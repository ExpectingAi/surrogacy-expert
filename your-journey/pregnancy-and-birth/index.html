Pregnancy & Birth

Pregnancy & Birth in Surrogacy: What Intended Parents Need to Know

Nine months of shared milestones. Here is how to be present, supportive, and prepared as your surrogate carries your child.

The First Trimester

After confirmed pregnancy, your surrogate continues with the fertility clinic until approximately 10–12 weeks, then transitions to her OB for routine prenatal care. Communication between you and your surrogate becomes the rhythm of this phase. Discuss in advance how often you want updates, whether you'll receive ultrasound photos and videos, and whether you'll attend any in-person appointments.

Staying Connected During the Pregnancy

Most intended parents and surrogates settle into a comfortable communication pattern — regular texts, video calls, sharing ultrasound images. Some attend major ultrasounds in person or virtually. The key is agreeing on these expectations before the pregnancy begins (ideally in the GCA) and then being flexible and gracious as the relationship evolves.

💡 Sending small, thoughtful care packages during the pregnancy — particularly in the third trimester when discomfort increases — is a gesture surrogates consistently describe as meaningful.

Birth Plan & Hospital Protocols

Work with your surrogate and attorney in the third trimester to prepare a hospital birth plan that reflects your wishes. Surrogacy-experienced hospitals (common in California, Nevada, Texas, Illinois, and other major surrogacy states) typically have established protocols for intended parents in the delivery room and on the postpartum floor. Confirm the hospital's surrogacy policies in advance.

At the Birth

Most surrogates want intended parents present at the birth, and most intended parents describe it as the most emotional and profound experience of their lives. Agree in advance on details: who will be in the room, who will cut the cord, who holds the baby first, and how the surrogate will be honored in that moment.

After the Birth: Establishing Parental Rights

If you have a pre-birth order, you are listed on the birth certificate immediately. If a post-birth legal process is required in your state, your attorney completes that within days or weeks of birth. Your baby is yours to take home.
