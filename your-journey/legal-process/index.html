    The Legal Process in Gestational Surrogacy: Contracts & Parental Rights

    Complete guide to surrogacy legal process — Gestational Carrier Agreements, pre-birth orders, parental rights establishment, and how laws vary by delivery state.

    Your Reproductive Attorney: The Most Important Professional on Your Team

    Hire a reproductive attorney before signing with an agency, before matching with a surrogate, and before beginning any medical procedures. Your attorney’s job is to protect your parental rights — which is the entire point of the legal process in surrogacy. The attorney you choose must be licensed in the state where your surrogate will deliver, and must have specific gestational surrogacy experience (not just general family law).

    Every dollar you spend on an experienced reproductive attorney is the highest-value legal expenditure in your entire surrogacy journey. This is not a place to economize.

    The Gestational Carrier Agreement (GCA)

    The GCA is the legal foundation of your surrogacy arrangement. It must be signed by all parties — each represented by independent legal counsel — before any fertility clinic will proceed with the embryo transfer. A complete GCA covers:

    • Surrogate’s compensation schedule and milestone payments
    • Expense reimbursements and allowances
    • Medical decision-making authority during the pregnancy
    • The number of embryos to be transferred
    • Protocols for selective reduction or termination in specific circumstances
    • Communication expectations during and after the pregnancy
    • Intended parents’ presence at appointments and at birth
    • How parental rights will be legally established
    • What happens in case of miscarriage, fetal abnormality, or surrogate’s health emergency

    GCA negotiation typically takes 3–6 weeks. Both parties’ attorneys negotiate the terms, draft, revise, and finalize the document before all parties sign. Intended parents typically pay both their own attorney fees and the surrogate’s attorney fees.

    Pre-Birth Orders (PBOs)

    In surrogacy-friendly states, intended parents file for a pre-birth order during the second trimester. A PBO is a court order that: establishes intended parents as the legal parents of the expected child, directs the hospital to list intended parents’ names on the birth certificate at birth, and eliminates the need for post-birth adoption proceedings. In most surrogacy-friendly states, PBOs are granted as a matter of routine for all family types.

    Post-Birth Parentage Proceedings

    In states that do not issue PBOs — or for specific family configurations — parental rights are established through post-birth court proceedings. These may include a parentage petition, stepparent adoption, or a second-parent adoption, depending on the state and the intended parents’ relationship to the child. Post-birth proceedings can take several weeks to several months and should be planned for before the birth occurs.

    The Birth Certificate

    With a PBO in place, intended parents are named on the original birth certificate issued at birth. Without a PBO, the birth certificate process varies by state. In some states, the surrogate and her husband (if married) are initially listed; in others, intended parents can be listed directly even without a PBO. Your attorney will advise on the specific birth certificate process in your delivery state.
