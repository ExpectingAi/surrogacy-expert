    The Surrogacy Legal Process: GCA, Pre-Birth Orders & Parentage

    How the legal side of surrogacy works — from the Gestational Carrier Agreement through pre-birth or post-birth parentage orders and what's on your birth certificate on day one.

    Step 1: Retaining Independent Legal Counsel

    Both intended parents and the surrogate must be represented by separate, independent reproductive attorneys — ideally licensed in the state where the surrogate will deliver. The intended parents typically pay both sets of attorney fees. Your attorney is your most important professional relationship in the entire journey: hire them before selecting an agency so they can review your agency agreement before you sign it.

    Step 2: The Gestational Carrier Agreement (GCA)

    After matching, your attorney and the surrogate's attorney negotiate and draft the Gestational Carrier Agreement. This is the comprehensive legal contract covering:

    • Surrogate base compensation and payment schedule
    • Expense reimbursements (medical, travel, childcare, clothing, etc.)
    • Medical decision-making authority during the pregnancy
    • Selective reduction and termination protocols
    • Number of embryo transfer attempts covered
    • Communication and contact expectations during and after pregnancy
    • How parental rights will be legally established
    • What happens in the event of miscarriage, surrogate withdrawal, or other contingencies

    No fertility clinic will schedule medical appointments until the GCA is fully signed by both parties. Negotiation typically takes 3–6 weeks.

    Step 3: Pre-Birth Orders (PBO)

    In surrogacy-friendly states, your attorney files a parentage petition with the court during your surrogate's second trimester — typically around 20 weeks. The court reviews the GCA and supporting documentation and issues a pre-birth order naming intended parents as the legal parents of the expected child. The hospital receives a copy and lists intended parents on the birth certificate at delivery. The surrogate's name does not appear.

    PBOs are available for all family configurations — different-sex couples, same-sex couples, single parents — in states with comprehensive surrogacy statutes (California, Nevada, New York, Illinois, New Jersey, Washington, Colorado, and others).

    Step 4: Post-Birth Parentage Orders

    In states where PBOs are not routinely available, a post-birth parentage proceeding is required. Your attorney files the parentage petition after birth. The court issues an amended birth certificate listing intended parents. This process typically takes several weeks to a few months. Until the order is finalized, carry documentation of your legal status when traveling with the baby.

    The Birth Certificate

    With a PBO: the birth certificate is issued with intended parents' names directly — no surrogate's name appears.
    With a post-birth order: the initial birth certificate may list the surrogate; an amended certificate is issued after the parentage proceeding finishes.

    Hire your reproductive attorney before you choose your agency. They can review your agency contract, advise on delivery state selection, and ensure your legal protections are in place from day one of the process.
