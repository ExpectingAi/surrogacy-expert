The Legal Process of Surrogacy for Intended Parents

The legal process protects both you and your surrogate throughout the journey. Here is what you need to know about contracts, parental rights, and state law.

Why the Legal Process Matters

The gestational carrier agreement (GCA) is the legal foundation of your surrogacy arrangement. It defines compensation, expectations, medical decision-making authority, the process for establishing parental rights, and contingency plans for complex scenarios. Without it, neither party has legal protection.

Separate Legal Representation

Both intended parents and the surrogate must be represented by separate, independent reproductive attorneys. This is not optional — it's a safeguard for both parties and a requirement of virtually all reputable agencies and fertility clinics. Intended parents typically pay for both their own legal fees and the surrogate's attorney fees.

The Gestational Carrier Agreement (GCA)

The GCA covers: surrogate compensation and payment schedule; expense reimbursements; medical decision-making during pregnancy; the number of embryos to be transferred; protocols for multiples, selective reduction, or termination; contact expectations during and after pregnancy; and the process for establishing parental rights.

💡 Negotiating the GCA can take 3–6 weeks. No medical procedures should begin before the contract is fully signed and the escrow account is funded.

Pre-Birth Orders (PBOs)

In most surrogacy-friendly states, intended parents can obtain a pre-birth order — a court order issued during the pregnancy that declares you the legal parents and directs the hospital to list you on the birth certificate at birth. This eliminates the need for adoption proceedings after birth.

States that routinely issue PBOs include California, Nevada, Washington, Colorado, Connecticut, Maine, New Hampshire, Rhode Island, New Jersey, Illinois, and New York. Other states require post-birth processes. See our state-by-state guide for details.

International Intended Parents

If you are not a US citizen or resident, your legal situation is more complex. You will need to work with a US reproductive attorney experienced in international IP arrangements, as well as legal counsel in your home country. The key questions are: how will your country recognize parentage established in the US, and what documentation will your child need to enter your home country?
