Starting Your Surrogacy Journey as Intended Parents

Every surrogacy journey begins with a single decision: to pursue it. Here is how to take that decision from intention to action — practically and emotionally.

Before You Call an Agency

The first step isn't calling an agency — it's educating yourself so you can ask the right questions when you do. Spend 2–4 weeks reading comprehensively about how surrogacy works, what it costs, how legal protections work in different states, and what the emotional experience is like for intended parents.

Key questions to answer before you start talking to agencies:

  • Do we have embryos ready, or do we need to create them first (possibly with donor eggs)?
  • Which state will our surrogate live and deliver in?
  • What is our total budget?
  • Do we want an agency, or are we considering an independent journey?
  • What communication style and relationship do we want with our surrogate?

Building Your Team

A surrogacy journey requires several professional relationships:

  • Surrogacy agency: Your central coordinator, surrogate matcher, and case manager. Not strictly required but strongly recommended.
  • Reproductive attorney: Handles your gestational carrier agreement and parental rights. Essential from day one.
  • Fertility clinic (REI): Manages IVF, embryo creation, and the transfer to your surrogate.
  • Mental health professional: Required by most agencies and clinics for both parties — and genuinely valuable.
  • Escrow company: Manages financial disbursements to your surrogate throughout the journey.

💡 Your reproductive attorney should be experienced specifically in the state where your surrogate will deliver. Reproductive law is highly state-specific, and experience matters enormously.

Setting Realistic Expectations

Surrogacy journeys rarely go exactly to plan. Embryo transfers may fail on the first attempt. Matching can take longer than expected. Legal timelines shift. Building in emotional and financial reserves for these scenarios protects you from the kind of crushing disappointment that derails many journeys. Most intended parents who complete their journey describe it as profoundly worth it — but they also describe it as harder than they expected.
