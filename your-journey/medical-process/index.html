    Medical Process of Gestational Surrogacy: IVF & Embryo Transfer

    Complete guide to the medical side of gestational surrogacy — embryo creation, surrogate medical screening, frozen embryo transfer protocol, and pregnancy confirmation.

    The Two Medical Tracks

    The medical process of gestational surrogacy runs on two parallel tracks: the intended parents’ track (creating the embryo) and the surrogate’s track (preparing her uterus for the embryo transfer). Both tracks are coordinated by the fertility clinic.

    Intended Parents’ Track: Creating the Embryo

    If intended parents have frozen embryos ready, this step is already complete. If not, one or both of the following IVF procedures may be required:

    • IVF with the intended mother’s eggs: The intended mother undergoes ovarian stimulation and egg retrieval. Her eggs are fertilized with the intended father’s or donor’s sperm in the laboratory. Resulting embryos are typically biopsied for PGT-A (preimplantation genetic testing) before being frozen.
    • Egg donation + IVF: When the intended mother cannot or does not want to use her own eggs, a donor’s eggs are used. Egg donation cycles are run simultaneously with the surrogate’s preparation, or frozen donor eggs are used.
    • Sperm donation: Single women, same-sex female couples, and any intended parents with male factor infertility may use donor sperm to fertilize the egg.

    Surrogate’s Medical Screening

    Before any transfer, your surrogate must pass a comprehensive medical screening at your fertility clinic:

    • Uterine assessment (saline sonogram or hysteroscopy) to confirm the uterine cavity is normal
    • Complete blood panel and infectious disease screening
    • BMI and general health review
    • Psychological evaluation (if not already completed by the agency)
    • Review of obstetric history and prior deliveries

    If the clinic approves her for transfer, the medication protocol begins. If the clinic has concerns, they may request additional testing or, in rare cases, decline to proceed.

    Frozen Embryo Transfer (FET) Protocol

    Most surrogacy transfers today are frozen embryo transfers (FET). The surrogate’s protocol typically includes:

    • Baseline monitoring: Blood tests and ultrasound on Day 1–2 of her cycle to confirm baseline hormone levels
    • Estrogen priming: Oral or patch-based estradiol for 10–14 days to thicken the uterine lining
    • Progesterone supplementation: Begun approximately 5 days before the planned transfer date
    • Lining check: Ultrasound confirms uterine lining thickness (≥7mm is generally required)
    • Transfer day: A catheter is used to place one or two embryos through the cervix into the uterine cavity. The procedure takes approximately 10 minutes and is typically painless.

    After the Transfer

    Your surrogate rests for the remainder of transfer day. Approximately 10–14 days later, a blood beta-hCG test confirms whether the transfer resulted in a pregnancy. A rising beta-hCG level confirmed by a second test 48 hours later indicates a successful implantation. An ultrasound at approximately 6–7 weeks confirms a heartbeat.

    First transfer success rates for gestational surrogacy using PGT-tested embryos typically range from 60–75%. Multiple transfers may be needed — plan emotionally and financially for this possibility.
