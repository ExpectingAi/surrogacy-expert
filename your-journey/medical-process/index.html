Medical Process

The Medical Process of Surrogacy: IVF & Embryo Transfer Guide

The medical process of gestational surrogacy involves your fertility clinic, your surrogate, and careful coordination. Here is exactly what happens from embryo creation to confirmed pregnancy.

Step 1: Creating the Embryos

If you haven't already, you'll work with a reproductive endocrinologist (REI) at a fertility clinic to create embryos. The process involves: ovarian stimulation and egg retrieval (for the intended mother or egg donor), sperm collection (from the intended father or sperm donor), fertilization in the lab, and embryo development to the blastocyst stage (day 5–6). Embryos may be genetically tested using preimplantation genetic testing (PGT) before being frozen.

Step 2: Surrogate Medical Screening

Before any transfer can occur, your surrogate must complete a comprehensive medical evaluation at your fertility clinic. This typically includes: uterine cavity evaluation (sonohysterogram or hysteroscopy), infectious disease testing, complete blood work, and review of prior obstetric records. The clinic must formally medically clear the surrogate before proceeding.

💡 Occasionally, a surrogate who passed an agency's initial screening does not pass the fertility clinic's medical screening. This is a known risk — most agencies have a policy for handling this situation, including re-matching without additional agency fees.

Step 3: Cycle Synchronization

Once medically cleared, your surrogate begins a hormonal protocol to prepare her uterine lining for implantation. This involves oral or injectable estrogen to thicken the lining, followed by progesterone starting a few days before the transfer. The protocol is monitored with blood tests and ultrasounds at a local monitoring clinic (affiliated with your primary IVF clinic).

Step 4: Embryo Transfer

The frozen embryo transfer (FET) is a simple, non-surgical procedure performed at your fertility clinic. A catheter is used to place the embryo into the surrogate's uterus. The procedure takes about 15 minutes. Your surrogate continues progesterone support for several weeks after the transfer.

Step 5: Pregnancy Confirmation

Approximately 10–14 days after the transfer, a blood beta-hCG test confirms pregnancy. A second test 48 hours later assesses whether levels are rising appropriately. If levels are rising, an ultrasound at 6–7 weeks confirms a heartbeat and number of embryos implanted. At approximately 10–12 weeks, care transfers from the fertility clinic to your surrogate's OB.
