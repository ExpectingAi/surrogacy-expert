How to Choose a Surrogacy Agency as Intended Parents

Your surrogacy agency will be your most important partner for the next 12–18 months. Choosing wisely is one of the highest-leverage decisions you'll make.

What Does a Surrogacy Agency Do?

A full-service surrogacy agency handles: surrogate recruitment, screening, and background checks; intended parent profiles and matching; case management and coordination; escrow services (sometimes); education and support; and crisis management if something goes wrong. The best agencies function as knowledgeable advocates throughout your entire journey.

Questions to Ask Every Agency

  • How long has the agency been in operation, and how many journeys has it completed?
  • What does the surrogate screening process include? (Background check, medical history, psychological evaluation, home study?)
  • How many active surrogates are in your pool, and what is average matching time?
  • Do you work with independent reproductive attorneys, or do you have in-house legal staff? (In-house legal often creates a conflict of interest.)
  • What happens if our surrogate needs to withdraw? What is your re-matching policy?
  • Do you manage escrow, or do you use a third-party service?
  • Can we speak with past intended parents who have completed journeys with your agency?

⚠️ Be cautious of agencies that guarantee short matching times, discourage you from seeking independent legal counsel, or are vague about their surrogate screening process. These are significant red flags.

Agency vs. Independent Surrogacy

Working without an agency — using a known surrogate or one found through community networks — can save $20,000–$35,000 in agency fees. But it also means you take on all coordination responsibilities yourself: vetting your surrogate, coordinating medical and legal appointments, managing communication, and troubleshooting problems. For most intended parents, particularly those pursuing surrogacy for the first time, agency support is well worth the cost.

Red Flags to Watch For

  • Pressure to sign before you've had time to review all documents carefully
  • Vague answers about how surrogates are screened
  • No references from past intended parents available
  • Unusually low fees without clear explanation of what is excluded
  • Claims of guaranteed success rates
  • In-house attorneys representing both you and the surrogate
