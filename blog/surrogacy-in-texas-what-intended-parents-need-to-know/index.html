    Surrogacy in Texas: What Intended Parents Need to Know

    Texas has a dedicated surrogacy statute and one of the largest surrogate communities in the country. But LGBTQ+ protections are limited, county selection matters, and the statutory requirements exclude some intended parent configurations. Here's what you need to know.

    Texas's Surrogacy Law: The Gestational Agreement Act

    Texas enacted the Gestational Agreement Act (Texas Family Code §§ 160.751–160.763) in 2003, making it one of the earlier states to codify gestational surrogacy in statute. The law authorizes gestational agreements, provides a framework for pre-birth parentage orders, and creates a clear process for validating surrogacy contracts in court.

    What the Texas statute requires

    The Texas GPAA requires that: (1) the gestational agreement be validated by a court before the embryo transfer; (2) the intended mother be unable to carry a pregnancy or it presents an unreasonable health risk; (3) at least one intended parent contribute gametes (genetic material); and (4) the agreement be reviewed and approved by the court in the county where the surrogate resides or where the birth is expected to occur.

    The court validation requirement is worth noting — unlike California or Nevada, where attorneys file for a parentage order mid-pregnancy without prior court approval of the contract itself, Texas requires court pre-validation of the gestational agreement. This adds a procedural step but also adds legal certainty once completed.

    Texas's Statutory Restrictions

    The Texas GPAA contains requirements that effectively exclude some intended parent configurations:

    • Gamete requirement: At least one intended parent must have a genetic connection to the child. This means fully donor-conceived embryos (donor egg + donor sperm) may not qualify under the statute. Your attorney may need to proceed under a case-law approach if neither intended parent contributes genetic material.
    • Medical necessity requirement: The intended mother must be medically unable to carry, or pregnancy must present an unreasonable health risk. This requirement is broadly interpreted in practice, but it means surrogacy as an elective choice (for career reasons, for example) may face scrutiny.
    • No explicit LGBTQ+ protections: The statute was written in 2003 and does not explicitly address same-sex couples or single parents. Texas courts have issued parentage orders for same-sex couples, but the legal framework is less certain than in explicitly inclusive states.

    Pre-Birth Orders in Texas

    Pre-birth orders are available in Texas, but the process is more involved than in statute-based states like California. The gestational agreement must be court-validated before any embryo transfer. After the transfer, the intended parents' parentage is confirmed through a separate order issued later in the pregnancy. In practice, experienced Texas reproductive attorneys manage this process smoothly in Harris County (Houston), Travis County (Austin), Dallas County, Bexar County (San Antonio), and Tarrant County (Fort Worth).

    LGBTQ+ Families and Texas Surrogacy

    Important: Texas's surrogacy statute does not explicitly protect same-sex couples or single LGBTQ+ parents. While Texas courts have issued parentage orders for same-sex couples — particularly in Austin, Houston, and Dallas — outcomes depend on the county and judge. LGBTQ+ families should consult carefully with a Texas-licensed reproductive attorney and seriously evaluate whether delivering in a neighboring state with explicit LGBTQ+ protections (New Mexico, Colorado) is logistically feasible.

    County Selection in Texas

    Texas is a large state with significant variation in court experience and judicial culture. Travis County (Austin), Harris County (Houston), Dallas County, and Bexar County (San Antonio) have the most surrogacy-experienced courts. Rural Texas counties have limited experience and may handle the court validation and parentage proceedings more slowly or with less familiarity. Work with your attorney to assess the specific county where your surrogate plans to deliver.

    Texas's Surrogacy Advantages

    Despite its complexity, Texas has real advantages for intended parents:

    • Large surrogate community: Texas has one of the largest surrogate pools in the country, particularly in the Houston, DFW, Austin, and San Antonio metros.
    • Lower compensation than coastal markets: Texas surrogates typically earn $32,000–$50,000, compared to $45,000–$65,000 in California. This meaningfully reduces the total journey cost.
    • Strong fertility clinic infrastructure: Multiple world-class fertility clinics operate in Houston, Dallas, and Austin.
    • Statutory framework: Despite its limitations, the Texas GPAA provides more legal certainty than pure case-law states.

    Texas Surrogacy Costs

    Total Texas surrogacy costs typically range from $90,000 to $160,000 — meaningfully lower than California for comparable journey quality. The lower surrogate compensation is the primary driver. Agency fees in Texas are similar to national norms ($25,000–$45,000). Legal fees may be slightly higher than in simpler states due to the court validation step, but not dramatically so.

    Texas is a workable surrogacy state for different-sex married couples meeting the statutory requirements. For LGBTQ+ families, the legal uncertainty is real — discuss your specific situation with a Texas reproductive attorney before committing to a Texas delivery.
