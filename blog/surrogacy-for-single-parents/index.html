    Surrogacy for Single Parents: A Complete Guide

    Single intended parents — men and women — are a growing group in the surrogacy world. The process is essentially the same as for couples, with a few important differences in genetics, legal strategy, and birth preparation.

    You Don't Need a Partner to Pursue Surrogacy

    Single parenthood by choice through surrogacy is increasingly common and completely achievable. The medical and legal processes are identical to those for couples — with the distinction that there is only one intended parent to establish on the birth certificate, and only one set of genetics to contribute (or supplement with a donor).

    Single Men: What You Need

    A single man pursuing surrogacy needs two things in addition to the standard journey components: an egg donor and a gestational surrogate (who are almost always different people). His sperm fertilizes the donor's eggs via IVF to create embryos, which are then transferred to the surrogate.

    In surrogacy-friendly states, a single male intended father can be named as the sole legal parent on the birth certificate from day one via a pre-birth order — no second parent, no surrogate's name. This is well-established in California, Nevada, Illinois, New York, and other statute-based states. In moderate states, some courts are less experienced with single-parent surrogacy; your attorney's experience specifically with single-father cases matters.

    Egg donor timing tip for single men

    You can often start your egg donor search in parallel with your agency and surrogate search. Creating embryos before matching with a surrogate means you enter the matching phase with embryos ready — shortening your total timeline by 2–3 months.

    Single Women: What You Need

    A single woman who cannot carry a pregnancy due to a medical condition can use her own eggs (fertilized with donor sperm via IVF) or donor eggs if needed. The resulting embryos are transferred to a gestational surrogate. As with single men, in surrogacy-friendly states the single intended mother can be the sole person named on the birth certificate from birth via a pre-birth order.

    The Single-Parent Legal Advantage

    One underappreciated aspect of single-parent surrogacy in friendly states: the PBO names only you. There is no second-parent question, no non-genetic parent issue, no partner whose rights need simultaneous establishment. From a parentage order perspective, single-parent surrogacy in a friendly state is often cleaner than same-sex couple surrogacy.

    In moderate states, the picture is more variable. Some courts are accustomed to couple surrogacy but have less experience with single-parent cases. Others handle both equally. Your attorney's specific single-parent surrogacy experience in your delivery county matters more than the state's general reputation.

    Finding the Right Agency

    Not all surrogacy agencies have extensive experience with single intended parents. When evaluating agencies, specifically ask: how many single-parent journeys have you completed in the last two years? Do single intended parents typically take longer to match than couples in your pool? (Some agencies have surrogates who prefer couples; others actively recruit single-parent-welcoming surrogates.) Agencies with strong LGBTQ+ practices tend to have better single-parent experience as well.

    Building Your Support Network Before the Birth

    One of the most important preparations for any single intended parent: build your support network before the birth, not after. Identify a primary backup caregiver who can step in when you're sick, traveling, or need a break. Arrange parental leave from work. Connect with the single-parents-by-choice community (SMBC Forum, PVED, Single Dads by Choice groups) — the peer support from people who've done exactly what you're doing is irreplaceable. The journey ahead is deeply doable. You won't be the first, and you won't be the last.

    Single-parent surrogacy in a surrogacy-friendly state is legally clean and increasingly well-supported by agencies. The biggest preparation isn't legal or medical — it's building the village you'll need once your child arrives.
