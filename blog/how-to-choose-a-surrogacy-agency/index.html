    How to Choose a Surrogacy Agency: What No One Tells You

    The questions you must ask before signing with any agency, the red flags experienced intended parents learned the hard way, and what the $25,000–$50,000 agency fee actually does — and doesn't — cover.

    The First Thing Most Intended Parents Get Wrong

    Most intended parents contact agencies before they've spoken with a reproductive attorney. This is backwards. An attorney can review any agency contract before you sign it, advise you on what's standard and what's not, and tell you whether the agency's legal partners are appropriate for your delivery state. A 30-minute attorney consultation before you sign with an agency can save you tens of thousands of dollars and months of problems.

    The second most common mistake: signing with the first agency that made you feel hopeful. Every agency's introductory call is designed to make you feel understood, supported, and optimistic. That's their job. The real test is what happens when things go wrong — and in surrogacy, things routinely go sideways. Ask about that.

    What an Agency Actually Does

    A full-service surrogacy agency recruits and pre-screens surrogate candidates, manages the matching process, coordinates communication between intended parents and their surrogate, oversees case management throughout the journey, and maintains established relationships with reproductive attorneys, fertility clinics, and escrow providers. Agencies do not: provide legal advice, draft your GCA, guarantee outcomes, or control the fertility clinic's medical decisions. Understanding this scope helps you evaluate what you're paying for.

    The 12 Questions to Ask Every Agency

    1. How many journeys have you completed, and how many are active right now?

    You want specific numbers. An agency that's completed 500 journeys over 15 years has different institutional knowledge than one that's completed 50 in 3 years. Both may be fine — but you should know which you're working with.

    2. Give me a complete, itemized fee schedule — in writing, before I sign.

    Not a range. Not a summary. The specific amounts and what triggers additional charges. Agency fees are frequently structured so the base fee sounds manageable but milestone-triggered add-ons significantly increase the total. Know the full picture upfront.

    3. What does your fee cover if my surrogate needs to withdraw and I need to rematch?

    Rematch is more common than agencies typically admit. Surrogates withdraw for all sorts of reasons — life changes, failed medical screening, personal circumstances. What happens to your money? Is rematch covered? Is there an additional fee? A reputable agency should have clear, fair rematch policies.

    4. Who specifically will be my case manager, and what's their caseload?

    Case manager quality varies enormously within the same agency. An overloaded case manager with 25 active cases handles things very differently from one with 12. Ask specifically who yours will be and what they're currently managing.

    5. Which escrow company do you use, and is it independent from your agency?

    This is a non-negotiable. The escrow company managing your surrogate's compensation must be completely independent from the agency. An agency that manages its own escrow has a conflict of interest that cannot be adequately mitigated. Full stop.

    6. How many surrogates are currently in your active, available pool?

    A large active pool means faster matching. A small pool means you might wait. Some agencies advertise large networks but have a much smaller actually-available-right-now pool. Ask the specific number of surrogates who are currently available for matching (not "in our network").

    7. What is your actual average matching timeline for someone in my situation?

    "2–4 months" is a common answer. Ask for specifics: what was the average for intended parents matched in the last 12 months? For LGBTQ+ families specifically, if applicable? Averages can hide wide variation.

    8. Can you connect me with three past intended parents I can speak to directly?

    A reputable agency will do this without hesitation. If they're reluctant or offer only written testimonials, that's a yellow flag. When you speak with references, ask specifically about what went wrong and how the agency handled it.

    9. Which reproductive attorneys do you work with, and in which states?

    The attorneys the agency refers you to will handle your most critical legal protections. Verify independently that these attorneys are experienced in the specific delivery state and have specific surrogacy (not just general family law) expertise.

    10. What fertility clinics do you work with, and can I use my own clinic?

    Some agencies have preferred clinic networks; others are clinic-agnostic. If you have an existing relationship with a clinic (or a strong preference), confirm upfront that the agency will work with them.

    11. How do you handle a surrogate who fails the fertility clinic's medical screening after we've matched?

    This happens more than people expect. Agency-level screening and clinic-level screening are separate — a surrogate who passes the agency's screening may be declined by the clinic. Ask what happens to your fees and timeline in that scenario.

    12. What does your contract say about refunds if I'm not satisfied?

    Most agency contracts are milestone-based — fees are earned as services are rendered. Very few agencies offer meaningful refunds once matching has begun. Understand exactly what's refundable before you sign.

    Red Flags to Walk Away From

    🚩 Pressure to sign quickly or "lock in your spot"

    Legitimate agencies don't create artificial urgency. "We have surrogates available right now but I can't hold them" is a sales tactic.

    🚩 The agency manages its own escrow

    As noted above — this is a structural conflict of interest that cannot be adequately mitigated. Independent escrow is non-negotiable.

    🚩 Vague or non-itemized fee schedules

    "Our fee is $35,000 and covers everything" without a detailed breakdown is a red flag. Request an itemized schedule and note every "additional" line.

    🚩 Guaranteed matching timelines

    No agency can guarantee a matching timeline. If they're willing to put it in writing, read the fine print very carefully about what constitutes a "match" and what happens if the timeline is missed.

    🚩 Reluctance to provide references

    A confident agency with satisfied past clients will connect you with them readily. Reluctance suggests either that clients weren't satisfied or that client relationships are guarded for other reasons.

    🚩 Legal advice from the agency itself

    Agencies are not attorneys and cannot provide legal advice. An agency that tells you "you don't need to retain your own attorney" or "our in-house legal team handles everything" is overstepping their lane and potentially exposing you to unacceptable risk.

    Higher Fee Does Not Mean Better Service

    This is the most important thing to internalize before your agency search. There is no direct correlation between agency fees and outcome quality, case manager experience, or surrogate vetting standards. Some of the most expensive agencies have had serious failures. Some mid-tier agencies have exceptional track records. Evaluate on substance: years in business, completed journey volume, reference quality, case manager experience, and specifically how they handled past problems.

    The single best thing you can do before choosing an agency: speak with a reproductive attorney first. They've seen the contracts, they know the agencies, and they'll tell you — confidentially — what they've encountered in practice.
