    Understanding Surrogacy

    How Much Does Surrogacy Cost? Complete 2025 Breakdown

    An honest, line-by-line breakdown of surrogacy costs for intended parents in the US — surrogate compensation, agency fees, IVF, legal costs, insurance, and total range.

    Total Cost Range

    Total costs for gestational surrogacy in the United States typically range from $80,000 to $180,000, depending on your state, agency, surrogate, and fertility clinic. California and major surrogacy markets trend higher; other states may be lower.

    Surrogate Compensation ($30,000–$60,000)

    The surrogate’s base compensation is the largest single line item. The range reflects experience (first-time surrogates typically earn less than experienced surrogates), state, and negotiated terms. Base pay is disbursed monthly over the pregnancy after a confirmed heartbeat. Additional payments include a post-delivery payment, embryo transfer fees ($500–$1,000 per transfer), and various milestone payments outlined in the GCA.

    Surrogate Expenses & Allowances

    Beyond base pay, surrogates receive reimbursement for: maternity clothing allowance (typically $750–$1,500), travel and mileage to medical appointments, childcare during appointments, lost wages for time missed from work, housekeeping assistance in late pregnancy, and a postpartum recovery allowance. All of these are detailed in the GCA before the journey begins.

    Agency Fees ($20,000–$35,000)

    Surrogacy agency fees cover surrogate recruitment and vetting, the matching process, case management throughout the journey, coordination with the fertility clinic and attorneys, and crisis support if complications arise. Fees vary significantly — a higher fee doesn’t always mean better service. Request a full fee schedule in writing before signing any agency agreement.

    Before signing with any agency, request an itemized breakdown of exactly what their fee covers — and what it doesn’t.

    IVF & Medical Costs ($15,000–$30,000)

    Medical costs depend significantly on whether you have frozen embryos ready or need to create them. If embryo creation (IVF with stimulation cycle) is required, expect $12,000–$20,000 for the IVF cycle alone. Frozen embryo transfer (FET) protocols for the surrogate typically run $3,000–$8,000 per transfer, including medications. If PGT-A genetic testing is required, add $3,000–$6,000.

    Legal Fees ($8,000–$15,000)

    Both parties must be represented by separate, independent reproductive attorneys. Intended parents typically pay their own attorney fees plus the surrogate’s attorney fees. A GCA (Gestational Carrier Agreement) typically costs $4,000–$8,000 for the drafting and negotiation phase. If the birth state requires a court filing for parental rights (rather than a pre-birth order), add another $2,000–$5,000.

    Health Insurance ($5,000–$30,000)

    This is one of the most variable line items. Some surrogates have existing health insurance that covers surrogate pregnancies — these policies must be carefully reviewed by a fertility insurance specialist before the match is finalized. If the surrogate’s policy doesn’t cover surrogate pregnancies (or doesn’t exist), a separate surrogacy-friendly policy must be purchased. Premiums and deductibles vary widely by state and insurer.

    Financing Surrogacy

    Most intended parents finance surrogacy through a combination of: personal savings, 401(k) or IRA withdrawals, home equity lines of credit, personal loans from lenders who specialize in fertility financing (CapexMD, New Life Fertility Finance), and employer fertility benefits (increasingly common at large employers). Some intended parents also raise funds through family contributions or crowdfunding.
