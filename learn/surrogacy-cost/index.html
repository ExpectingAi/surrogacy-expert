    Understanding Surrogacy

    How Much Does Surrogacy Cost? Complete 2025 Breakdown

    An honest, line-by-line breakdown of surrogacy costs for intended parents in the US — surrogate compensation, agency fees, IVF, legal costs, insurance, and total range.

    Total Cost Overview

    There is no single number for surrogacy costs because every journey is different. Your total will depend on your state, your agency, your surrogate's compensation and health insurance situation, how many IVF cycles are needed, and your legal team's fees. That said, most intended parents should budget somewhere between $90,000 and $200,000.

    Surrogate Compensation ($30,000–$60,000)

    Surrogate base compensation is the largest single line item. It reflects experience, state, and what you negotiate in the GCA. First-time surrogates typically earn toward the lower end; surrogates with one or more prior journeys command higher fees. Monthly installments begin after a confirmed heartbeat and continue through delivery, with additional milestone payments for events like embryo transfer and birth. See our complete surrogate compensation guide for a full breakdown of base pay, milestones, and allowances.

    Surrogate Expenses & Allowances

    Beyond base pay, surrogates receive reimbursement for: maternity clothing (typically $750–$1,500), travel and mileage to medical appointments, childcare during appointments, lost wages for time missed from work, housekeeping assistance during bed rest, and a postpartum recovery allowance. All amounts are detailed in the Gestational Carrier Agreement before the journey begins.

    Agency Fees ($25,000–$50,000)

    Full-service surrogacy agency fees cover surrogate recruitment and vetting, the matching process, case management throughout the journey, coordination with the fertility clinic and attorneys, and crisis support if complications arise. Premium agencies — particularly those in California and major surrogacy markets — often charge toward the higher end of this range. Request a complete, itemized fee schedule in writing before signing. See our guide on how to choose a surrogacy agency.

    Before signing with any agency, request an itemized breakdown of exactly what their fee covers — and what it doesn't.

    IVF & Medical Costs ($15,000–$30,000)

    Medical costs depend on whether you have frozen embryos ready or need to create them. A full IVF stimulation cycle to create embryos typically costs $12,000–$20,000. The surrogate's frozen embryo transfer (FET) protocol runs $3,000–$6,000 per transfer including medications. If PGT-A genetic testing is needed, add $3,000–$6,000. See our medical process guide for a complete walkthrough.

    Legal Fees ($8,000–$15,000)

    Both parties must be represented by separate, independent reproductive attorneys. Intended parents pay their own attorney fees plus the surrogate's attorney fees. Drafting and negotiating the GCA typically costs $4,000–$8,000. The delivery state determines whether a court filing is needed for parental rights — in surrogacy-friendly states, a pre-birth order is routine. In others, add $2,000–$5,000 for post-birth proceedings. See our legal process guide.

    Health Insurance ($5,000–$30,000)

    This is one of the most variable line items. If your surrogate has existing insurance that covers surrogate pregnancies, intended parents typically pay her premiums during the journey. If her policy doesn’t cover surrogacy, a surrogacy-specific policy must be purchased. Always have a fertility insurance specialist review the surrogate's policy before finalizing a match.

    How State Choice Affects Total Cost

    Where your surrogate delivers affects both your legal costs and your surrogate's compensation. California surrogates typically command $10,000–$20,000 more than surrogates in Midwest or Southern states. A California delivery also provides the strongest legal protections but tends toward the higher end of the total budget range. Moderate states like Ohio, Georgia, or North Carolina often yield lower total costs with only modestly more legal complexity.

    Financing Surrogacy

    Most intended parents finance surrogacy through a combination of personal savings, 401(k) or IRA withdrawals, home equity lines of credit, personal loans from fertility-focused lenders, and employer fertility benefits.
