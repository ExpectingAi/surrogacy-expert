FAQ & Glossary

Surrogacy FAQ & Glossary for Intended Parents

Everything you wanted to know about surrogacy — answered clearly and completely. Plus a full glossary of terms you will encounter on your journey.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can intended parents be at the birth?

Yes, in most cases. Presence at the birth is one of the most important topics to discuss with your surrogate during the matching process, and it should be addressed in the Gestational Carrier Agreement. Most surrogates welcome intended parents in the delivery room or at least in the hospital. Surrogacy-experienced hospitals (common in California, Nevada, Texas, and Illinois) have established protocols for intended parents in the delivery room and on the postpartum floor.

What if the surrogate changes her mind?

In gestational surrogacy, the surrogate has no genetic connection to the baby, which significantly reduces this risk compared to traditional surrogacy. In states with strong surrogacy statutes, a pre-birth order establishes intended parents as legal parents before birth — meaning the surrogate has no legal path to claim the child. This scenario is extremely rare in properly structured gestational surrogacy arrangements.

What happens if the surrogate miscarries?

A miscarriage is a heartbreaking experience for everyone involved. The surrogate typically receives a miscarriage payment outlined in the GCA (usually $1,000–$2,500 depending on the stage). If the pregnancy is lost before 12 weeks and the surrogate is willing to try again, another embryo transfer cycle can be attempted after medical clearance. Your GCA should specify how many transfer attempts are included.

What is an escrow account in surrogacy?

A neutral third-party escrow company holds and manages all surrogacy funds — surrogate compensation, expense reimbursements, and agency-directed payments — on behalf of intended parents. Funds are released according to the payment schedule in the GCA. Reputable agencies require escrow management by an independent company, not the agency itself.

Do we need a surrogacy attorney even if we're using an agency?

Yes, absolutely. Your agency handles matching and case management — your attorney handles legal protection. Every intended parent must retain their own licensed reproductive attorney, and the surrogate must have separate independent legal representation (her attorney fees are typically paid by the intended parents).

Can we choose the sex of the baby through surrogacy?

Yes, if embryos are created via IVF and undergo preimplantation genetic testing (PGT-A), the genetic sex of each embryo can be determined. Intended parents can then choose which embryos to transfer based on sex, if desired. Note that not all fertility clinics offer sex selection for non-medical reasons.

Can international intended parents pursue surrogacy in the US?

Yes. The United States is one of the world's most popular surrogacy destinations for international intended parents. There is no residency requirement for surrogacy in any US state. International IPs do face additional complexity around their child's citizenship and immigration back to their home country. See our guide for international intended parents.

Surrogacy Glossary

Gestational Carrier (GC)

The woman who carries and delivers a baby for intended parents via gestational surrogacy. She has no genetic connection to the child. Also called a surrogate or gestational surrogate.

Intended Parents (IPs)

The person or people who will be the legal and social parents of the child born through surrogacy. They may or may not be genetically related to the child.

Gestational Carrier Agreement (GCA)

The comprehensive legal contract between intended parents and their gestational surrogate, covering all terms of the arrangement. Must be signed by both parties — each with independent legal counsel — before any medical procedures.

Pre-Birth Order (PBO)

A court order issued during pregnancy that names intended parents as the legal parents of the expected child, directing the hospital to list them on the birth certificate at birth. Available in most surrogacy-friendly states.

Frozen Embryo Transfer (FET)

The procedure in which a previously frozen embryo is thawed and transferred to the surrogate's uterus. Most surrogacy arrangements today use FET rather than fresh transfers.

Preimplantation Genetic Testing (PGT)

Genetic testing of embryos created via IVF prior to transfer, used to screen for chromosomal abnormalities (PGT-A), single-gene diseases (PGT-M), or to identify sex. PGT can reduce the risk of miscarriage and increase the likelihood of a successful transfer.

Escrow

A neutral third-party account managed by an independent escrow company that holds and disburses all surrogacy-related funds (compensation, expenses, insurance) according to the GCA payment schedule.

Reproductive Endocrinologist (REI)

A physician specializing in fertility and reproductive hormones who manages the IVF process — including embryo creation and the surrogate's transfer cycle.

Beta-hCG Test

A blood test measuring the pregnancy hormone hCG, performed approximately 10–14 days after embryo transfer to confirm pregnancy. Rising levels in a follow-up test 48 hours later indicate a healthy early pregnancy.

Independent Surrogacy

A surrogacy arrangement in which intended parents find and work with a surrogate directly, without using a surrogacy agency. Requires intended parents to manage all coordination themselves.

Selective Reduction

A medical procedure to reduce the number of fetuses in a multiple pregnancy. Both parties' positions on this must be addressed in the GCA before pregnancy.

Egg Donor

A woman who provides eggs for IVF, used when the intended mother cannot or chooses not to use her own eggs. Required for same-sex male couples and single men pursuing surrogacy.
