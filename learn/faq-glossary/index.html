FAQ & Glossary

Surrogacy FAQ & Glossary for Intended Parents

Everything you wanted to know about surrogacy — answered clearly and honestly. Plus a complete glossary of terms you'll encounter on your journey.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can intended parents be present at the birth?

Yes, in most cases. This is typically discussed and agreed upon in the gestational carrier agreement. Most surrogates welcome intended parents in the delivery room, or at least at the hospital. The hospital's policies also play a role — surrogacy-experienced hospitals (common in California, Texas, Nevada, and Illinois) often have established protocols for intended parents.

What happens if the surrogate changes her mind?

In gestational surrogacy, the surrogate has no genetic connection to the baby, which significantly reduces this risk compared to traditional surrogacy. In states with solid surrogacy statutes, the pre-birth order establishes intended parents as the legal parents before birth, meaning the surrogate has no legal path to keep the child. Thorough surrogate screening and a well-drafted GCA are your best protections.

Do we have to use our own eggs and sperm?

No. Many intended parents use donor eggs, donor sperm, or both. The embryo is still transferred to the surrogate, who carries the pregnancy but has no genetic connection to the child (when donor eggs are used).

What is an escrow account in surrogacy?

A third-party escrow service holds the surrogate's compensation and expense funds on behalf of intended parents. As the journey progresses, the escrow agent disburses funds according to the schedule outlined in the GCA. This protects both parties — the surrogate knows funds are secured, and intended parents maintain accountability over disbursements.

What is a pre-birth order (PBO)?

A pre-birth order is a court order obtained before the baby is born that declares the intended parents as the legal parents of the child. It allows their names to be placed on the birth certificate immediately at birth. PBOs are available in most surrogacy-friendly states and are one of the key legal advantages of choosing a favorable surrogacy jurisdiction.

Surrogacy Glossary

Gestational Carrier (GC)

The woman who carries and delivers the baby for the intended parents. She has no genetic relationship to the child in gestational surrogacy.

Intended Parents (IPs)

The person or people who will be the legal and social parents of the child. They may or may not be genetically related to the child.

Gestational Carrier Agreement (GCA)

The legal contract between intended parents and the surrogate, outlining all terms, compensation, expectations, and parental rights. Also called a surrogacy contract or surrogacy agreement.

Pre-Birth Order (PBO)

A court order issued before birth establishing intended parents as the legal parents and directing the birth certificate to list their names.

Embryo Transfer (ET)

The procedure in which a fertilized embryo is placed into the surrogate's uterus. In most journeys today, this is a frozen embryo transfer (FET).

Frozen Embryo Transfer (FET)

An embryo transfer using embryos that were previously frozen, as opposed to fresh embryos transferred immediately after fertilization.

Egg Donor

A woman who provides eggs for IVF, used when the intended mother cannot or does not wish to use her own eggs. The egg donor has no parental rights.

Escrow

A neutral third-party account that holds and disburses surrogate compensation and expense payments throughout the journey.

Independent Journey

A surrogacy arrangement conducted without an agency — typically involving a known surrogate or one found through surrogacy communities. Requires more self-coordination but can reduce costs.
