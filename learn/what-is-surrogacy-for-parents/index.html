Understanding Surrogacy

What Is Surrogacy for Intended Parents?

Gestational surrogacy is the process by which a woman — the surrogate — carries and delivers a baby for another person or couple, the intended parents. The baby is not genetically related to the surrogate.

Gestational Surrogacy: The Basics

In gestational surrogacy, an embryo created via in vitro fertilization (IVF) is transferred to the uterus of the surrogate, also called the gestational carrier. The embryo may be created using the intended mother's eggs and the intended father's sperm, or it may involve donor eggs, donor sperm, or both. Either way, the surrogate has no genetic connection to the baby she carries.

This is the most important distinction from traditional surrogacy, in which the surrogate's own egg is used — making her the genetic mother. Traditional surrogacy is rarely practiced today because of the legal and emotional complexities that arise from that genetic connection.

💡 Over 95% of surrogacy journeys in the United States today are gestational surrogacies, not traditional surrogacies.

Who Pursues Surrogacy?

Intended parents are diverse. Common reasons people pursue gestational surrogacy include:

  • Medical conditions that prevent a safe pregnancy — including absent or abnormal uterus (such as MRKH syndrome), significant heart or kidney disease, or other conditions where pregnancy poses a serious health risk
  • Repeated IVF failure or recurrent pregnancy loss, where embryo quality is good but implantation repeatedly fails
  • Same-sex male couples who need both an egg donor and a surrogate to have a biological child
  • Single men who wish to become parents
  • Transgender individuals who have undergone gender-affirming surgery that affects fertility
  • Single women or female couples who cannot safely carry a pregnancy

What Is a Gestational Carrier Agreement?

Before any medical procedures begin, intended parents and their surrogate must execute a gestational carrier agreement (GCA) — a detailed legal contract drafted and reviewed by separate attorneys for each party. This document outlines compensation, expectations, decision-making authority during pregnancy, and how parental rights will be established.

The GCA is one of the most critical documents in any surrogacy journey. It is legally required before the embryo transfer in virtually all reputable arrangements.

What Role Do Intended Parents Play?

As intended parents, you are the driving force behind the journey. You choose your agency (or go independent), provide the embryos (or arrange for donor material), select and approve your surrogate, execute the legal contract, pay the associated costs, attend key medical appointments, and ultimately receive the baby at birth.

Your surrogate carries the pregnancy, attends medical appointments, follows the medical protocol, and communicates with you throughout. Most intended parents describe maintaining a warm, respectful relationship with their surrogate as one of the most important parts of the journey.

Gestational vs. Traditional Surrogacy

  • Gestational surrogacy: Surrogate has no genetic connection to the baby. Embryo created via IVF. Most common. Cleaner legal landscape.
  • Traditional surrogacy: Surrogate is also the egg donor and genetic mother. Rarely practiced today. Significantly more complex legally and emotionally.

Is Surrogacy Legal?

In the United States, gestational surrogacy is legal in most states — but the laws governing it vary significantly. Some states have specific statutes, some operate on case law, and a few restrict or prohibit commercial surrogacy.
