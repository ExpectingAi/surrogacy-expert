Timeline & Process

Gestational Surrogacy Timeline: Month-by-Month Guide

A typical gestational surrogacy journey takes 12 to 18 months from starting your agency search to the birth of your child. Here is what to expect at each stage.

Phase 1: Research & Agency Selection (Months 1–2)

Most journeys begin with 4–8 weeks of dedicated research: understanding the process, evaluating costs, learning about the legal landscape in different states, and consulting with a reproductive attorney. During this phase you should also get your finances in order and begin conversations with 2–3 agencies before making a selection.

Key milestones: Choose your agency, complete your intended parent application, and pay your agency retainer fee.

Phase 2: Matching with a Surrogate (Months 2–5)

After your application is approved, your agency creates your intended parent profile and presents it to available surrogates. You review surrogate profiles — including background check results, medical history, psychological evaluation summaries, and personal letters — and express interest in candidates you'd like to meet. Most intended parents match within 1–4 months.

💡 The matching phase is emotionally the most variable. Some intended parents match in days; others wait months. Be patient — the right match matters far more than a fast one.

Phase 3: Legal Contracts (Months 5–6)

After matching, both parties retain independent reproductive attorneys who negotiate and draft the Gestational Carrier Agreement. This process typically takes 3–6 weeks. No medical procedures begin until the GCA is fully executed and the surrogate's escrow account is funded.

Phase 4: Medical Screening & IVF (Months 6–8)

Your surrogate undergoes a comprehensive medical evaluation at your fertility clinic: uterine cavity assessment, infectious disease testing (FDA-required), complete blood panel, and review of all prior obstetric records. Once cleared, she begins a hormonal preparation protocol for embryo transfer. A blood beta-hCG test 10–14 days after transfer confirms pregnancy.

Phase 5: Pregnancy (Months 8–17)

After confirmed pregnancy, your surrogate transitions from the fertility clinic to her OB at approximately 10–12 weeks. Intended parents attend key ultrasounds (12-week NT scan, 20-week anatomy scan) in person or virtually. At approximately 28–32 weeks, your reproductive attorney files for the pre-birth order. You prepare for the birth: confirm the hospital, finalize travel plans, and prepare your home.

Phase 6: Birth & Going Home (Month 17–18)

Your surrogate delivers at the hospital. If a pre-birth order was issued, you are named on the birth certificate immediately. The baby is yours to take home — and your journey as parents begins.

What Can Extend the Timeline?

  • A failed embryo transfer requiring a repeat cycle (adds 2–3 months)
  • Difficulty matching (can add 1–6 months depending on preferences)
  • Surrogate medical disqualification after agency screening (adds 4–8 weeks for re-matching)
  • Legal contract negotiations taking longer than expected
  • Complications during pregnancy requiring additional monitoring

Building emotional and financial contingencies for these possibilities is one of the most important forms of preparation for any intended parent.
