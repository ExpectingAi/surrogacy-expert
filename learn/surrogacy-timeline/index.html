Surrogacy Timeline for Intended Parents: Month by Month

A typical gestational surrogacy journey takes 12 to 18 months from starting your agency search to birth. Here is what to expect at every stage.

Phase 1: Research & Agency Selection (Months 1–2)

Most journeys begin with 4–8 weeks of research — understanding the process, costs, legal landscape, and agency options. During this phase you should: read broadly, speak with multiple agencies, consult with a reproductive attorney in your state, and get your finances in order. End this phase by selecting your agency and completing your application.

Phase 2: Profile & Matching (Months 2–5)

After your agency application is approved, you create an intended parent profile that your agency shares with potential surrogates. You'll review surrogate profiles — including background checks, medical history, psychological evaluations, and personal statements — and express interest in candidates you'd like to meet. Matching typically takes 1–4 months. Once both parties agree, you're officially matched.

💡 The matching phase is the most emotionally variable part of the journey. Some intended parents match within weeks; others wait months. Your agency should set realistic expectations based on your preferences and surrogate availability in your target state.

Phase 3: Legal Process (Months 5–6)

After matching, both parties retain separate reproductive attorneys who negotiate and draft the gestational carrier agreement. This process typically takes 3–6 weeks. No medical procedures begin until the contract is fully executed and the surrogate's escrow account is funded.

Phase 4: Medical Screening & IVF (Months 6–8)

Your surrogate completes a comprehensive medical screening at the fertility clinic, including a uterine evaluation, infectious disease testing, and psychological clearance. If she passes, her cycle is synchronized with the embryo transfer protocol. A frozen embryo transfer (FET) typically occurs 4–8 weeks after medical clearance. Pregnancy confirmation is approximately 10 days after transfer.

Phase 5: Pregnancy (Months 8–17)

Once pregnancy is confirmed, your surrogate transitions from the fertility clinic to an OB for routine prenatal care. Intended parents typically attend major ultrasounds and are in regular communication with their surrogate. During the third trimester, work with your attorney to file for a pre-birth order (in states that allow them). Begin preparing for your baby's arrival.

Phase 6: Birth & Going Home (Months 17–18)

Your surrogate delivers at the hospital. If a pre-birth order was issued, you are named on the birth certificate immediately. If a post-birth process is required in your state, your attorney completes that after delivery. Most hospitals in surrogacy-friendly states are experienced with the process and have protocols for intended parents. You take your baby home shortly after birth.
