    Understanding Surrogacy

    The Gestational Surrogacy Timeline: Month-by-Month Guide

    A realistic month-by-month timeline of the gestational surrogacy process for intended parents — from agency search through matching, IVF, pregnancy, and birth. Total: 12–18 months.

    Phase 1: Research & Agency Selection (Months 1–2)

    Before contacting any agency, spend 4–8 weeks educating yourself on the process, costs, and state laws. Evaluate 2–3 agencies. Consult a reproductive attorney independently — before signing any agency agreement. Once you select an agency, complete your intended parent profile and begin the agency onboarding process.

    Phase 2: Surrogate Matching (Months 2–6)

    Matching timelines vary significantly — from 4 weeks for well-matched intended parents with flexible preferences, to 4+ months for those with specific requirements. Most agencies report average matching times of 1–4 months. During this phase: your agency presents surrogate profiles, you review and meet candidates, and you choose a surrogate. Once matched, both parties sign a match agreement and legal contract negotiations begin.

    Phase 3: Legal Contracts (Weeks 4–6 After Matching)

    The Gestational Carrier Agreement (GCA) is negotiated and executed by independent attorneys for both parties. This process typically takes 3–6 weeks. No medical procedures can begin before the GCA is fully signed by all parties. During this time, your escrow account is established and initial funding is deposited.

    Phase 4: Medical Clearance & Embryo Transfer (Months 6–9)

    Your surrogate undergoes a comprehensive medical evaluation at your fertility clinic, including uterine assessment, infectious disease screening, and reproductive endocrinologist review. If she clears, the embryo transfer preparation begins: a mock transfer cycle (sometimes) followed by the real FET protocol, which takes approximately 6–8 weeks. The embryo transfer itself takes about 10 minutes.

    Phase 5: Pregnancy Confirmation (Weeks 10–14)

    Approximately 10–14 days after the transfer, your surrogate has a beta-hCG blood test. Rising levels confirm pregnancy. A second beta 48 hours later confirms viability. At 6–7 weeks, the first ultrasound confirms heartbeat. At approximately 10 weeks, your surrogate “graduates” from the fertility clinic to her OB. This is when intended parents often share the news.

    Phase 6: Pregnancy (Months 9–17)

    The pregnancy progresses like any pregnancy, with your surrogate managing her prenatal care. Intended parents typically attend key appointments (anatomy scan at 20 weeks, third-trimester appointments). During the second trimester, your reproductive attorney files for a pre-birth order. In the third trimester, intended parents prepare: arranging hospital access, preparing the nursery, coordinating travel to the delivery location.

    Phase 7: Birth & Legal Finalization (Month 17–18)

    Your baby is born. With a pre-birth order in place, you are immediately recognized as legal parents and named on the birth certificate. If a post-birth process is required (some states), your attorney files parentage paperwork within days of birth. You bring your baby home.

    Build 3–6 months of additional buffer into any timeline estimate. Multiple embryo transfers, delays in matching, or a legal complication can extend the journey — and that’s normal.
