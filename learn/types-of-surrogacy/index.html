Understanding Surrogacy

Types of Surrogacy: Gestational, Traditional & Independent

Not all surrogacy arrangements are the same. Understanding the key distinctions — gestational vs. traditional, agency vs. independent, compensated vs. altruistic — helps you make the right choices for your family.

Gestational Surrogacy

In gestational surrogacy, the surrogate carries an embryo created via IVF. She has no genetic relationship to the child. This is the standard approach in the United States today, accounting for the vast majority of arrangements. It is legally cleaner and medically more complex than traditional surrogacy.

Traditional Surrogacy

In traditional surrogacy, the surrogate is also the egg donor — making her the genetic mother of the child. This approach has largely fallen out of favor because it creates significant legal complexity around parental rights, particularly if the surrogate changes her mind. Most agencies, fertility clinics, and attorneys in the US no longer facilitate traditional surrogacy.

⚠️ If you encounter an arrangement described as "traditional surrogacy," consult a reproductive attorney before proceeding. The legal risks are substantially higher than in gestational surrogacy.

Agency vs. Independent (Private) Surrogacy

Agency surrogacy means working with a professional surrogacy agency that handles surrogate recruitment, screening, matching, case management, and coordination between all parties. Agencies typically charge $20,000–$35,000 for their services, but provide significant infrastructure, support, and risk management.

Independent surrogacy (also called private or DIY surrogacy) involves intended parents finding and working directly with a surrogate — usually someone they know or found through a surrogacy community — without an agency middleman. This can reduce costs but requires intended parents to take on significant coordination responsibilities and carries more risk if things go wrong.

Compensated vs. Altruistic Surrogacy

In compensated (commercial) surrogacy — the norm in the US — the surrogate receives a base fee for her time and commitment, in addition to reimbursement for all pregnancy-related expenses. Base compensation typically ranges from $30,000 to $60,000.

In altruistic surrogacy, the surrogate carries only for expense reimbursement, with no additional payment. This is more common in countries like Canada, Australia, and the UK where commercial surrogacy is legally restricted. It is rare in the United States but does occur, most often when the surrogate is a family member or close friend.

Domestic vs. International Surrogacy

US-based surrogacy offers a mature legal framework, experienced professionals, and strong enforceability of contracts. International surrogacy has become significantly more complicated as many countries (India, Thailand, Cambodia, Mexico) have restricted or banned commercial surrogacy for foreigners. We generally recommend US-based arrangements for their legal clarity and ethical standards.
