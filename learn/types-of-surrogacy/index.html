Understanding Surrogacy

Types of Surrogacy: What Intended Parents Need to Know

Not all surrogacy arrangements are the same. Understanding the key distinctions helps you choose the right path for your family.

Gestational vs. Traditional Surrogacy

Gestational surrogacy is the standard arrangement in the United States. The surrogate carries an embryo created via IVF — she has no genetic connection to the child. The embryo may be created with the intended parents' own genetic material, or with donor eggs, donor sperm, or both. Gestational surrogacy accounts for over 95% of all US surrogacy arrangements.

Traditional surrogacy involves the surrogate's own egg, making her the genetic mother of the child. This creates significant legal complexity: if the surrogate later wishes to assert parental rights, the genetic connection strengthens her legal standing in many states. Most US agencies, fertility clinics, and reproductive attorneys no longer facilitate traditional surrogacy.

⚠️ Traditional surrogacy carries substantially higher legal risk for intended parents than gestational surrogacy. In almost all cases, gestational surrogacy is the right choice.

Agency vs. Independent (Private) Surrogacy

Agency surrogacy means working with a professional surrogacy agency that manages surrogate recruitment, background screening, psychological evaluation, matching, case management, and coordination between your legal, medical, and financial teams. Agencies typically charge $20,000–$35,000.

Independent surrogacy involves finding and working directly with a surrogate — typically someone you already know, or someone found through a surrogacy community or online matching platform — without an agency intermediary. This can save $20,000–$35,000 in agency fees but requires you to manage all coordination yourself.

For most first-time intended parents, agency surrogacy is strongly recommended.

Compensated vs. Altruistic Surrogacy

Compensated (commercial) surrogacy is the norm in the United States. The surrogate receives a base fee for her commitment, time, and physical contribution — typically $30,000–$60,000 — in addition to full reimbursement for all pregnancy-related expenses. This is legal in most US states.

Altruistic surrogacy involves a surrogate who receives only expense reimbursement, with no additional base compensation. In the US, altruistic arrangements most often occur when the surrogate is a close friend or family member.

Domestic vs. International Surrogacy

The United States offers the most mature and legally secure surrogacy environment in the world. Pre-birth orders, enforceable contracts, experienced professionals, and high IVF success rates make it the top destination for both domestic and international intended parents.

International surrogacy outside the US has become significantly more restricted. India, Thailand, Cambodia, and Nepal have banned commercial surrogacy for foreigners. For most intended parents, pursuing surrogacy within the United States is the safest, most legally secure option.

Which Type Is Right for You?

For the vast majority of intended parents: gestational surrogacy through a licensed agency, with a compensated surrogate, in a surrogacy-friendly US state. This combination gives you the strongest legal protections, the most professional support, and the highest likelihood of a smooth journey.
