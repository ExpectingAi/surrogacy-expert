    Understanding Surrogacy

    Types of Surrogacy: Gestational, Traditional, Agency & Independent

    The key distinctions in surrogacy arrangements — gestational vs traditional, agency vs independent, compensated vs altruistic — and what each means for intended parents.

    Gestational vs. Traditional Surrogacy

    The most important distinction in any surrogacy arrangement is whether the surrogate has a genetic connection to the baby she carries.

    Gestational Surrogacy

    In gestational surrogacy — the standard form in the US today — the surrogate carries an embryo created via IVF using the intended parents’ or donors’ genetic material. The surrogate has no genetic connection to the baby. This distinction is legally critical: because the surrogate is not the genetic mother, courts in surrogacy-friendly states can issue pre-birth orders establishing intended parents’ parental rights before birth, without adoption proceedings.

    Traditional Surrogacy

    In traditional surrogacy, the surrogate is inseminated with sperm (from the intended father or a donor), meaning she is the genetic mother of the child she carries. Traditional surrogacy carries significant legal and emotional risks: because the surrogate is the genetic mother, her parental rights are more complex to terminate. Most US surrogacy agencies, fertility clinics, and reproductive attorneys no longer facilitate traditional surrogacy arrangements.

    Over 95% of surrogacy arrangements in the US today are gestational. If you encounter an offer of traditional surrogacy, consult a reproductive attorney before proceeding.

    Agency Surrogacy vs. Independent Surrogacy

    Agency-Assisted Surrogacy

    Agency surrogacy means working with a professional surrogacy agency that manages surrogate recruitment, background screening, psychological evaluation, matching, case management, and coordination between your legal, medical, and financial teams. Agencies typically charge $25,000–$50,000, but provide significant infrastructure, risk management, and peace of mind.

    Independent Surrogacy

    Independent surrogacy (also called private or DIY surrogacy) involves finding and working directly with a surrogate — typically someone you already know, or someone found through a surrogacy community or online matching platform — without an agency intermediary. This can save $25,000–$50,000 in agency fees but requires you to manage all coordination yourself and carries more risk if complications arise.

    Compensated vs. Altruistic Surrogacy

    Compensated surrogacy: The surrogate receives base compensation beyond expense reimbursement. This is legal and standard practice in the United States. Surrogate base compensation typically ranges from $30,000 to $60,000.

    Altruistic surrogacy: The surrogate receives only expense reimbursement, no base fee. Common among close friends or family members serving as surrogates. Required by law in some countries (Canada, UK, Australia) but not in the US.

    Known vs. Unknown (Agency-Matched) Surrogates

    Known surrogate: A friend, family member, or acquaintance who agrees to carry for the intended parents. Can simplify the matching process and reduce agency fees, but requires careful attention to legal and psychological groundwork to protect the relationship.

    Agency-matched surrogate: A surrogate recruited through an agency’s pool of candidates. The most common path for first-time intended parents who don’t have an existing relationship with a potential surrogate.

    Domestic vs. International Surrogacy

    International intended parents pursuing surrogacy in the United States must navigate two legal systems: US law (establishing parentage in the delivery state) and their home country’s law (recognizing those parental rights and conferring citizenship or residency on the child). The US is one of the world’s most popular surrogacy destinations for international intended parents because of its strong legal framework and high IVF success rates.
