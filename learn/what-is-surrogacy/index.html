Understanding Surrogacy

What Is Gestational Surrogacy for Intended Parents?

Gestational surrogacy is the process by which a woman — the surrogate — carries and delivers a baby for another person or couple. The baby is not genetically related to the surrogate.

What Is Gestational Surrogacy?

In gestational surrogacy, an embryo created via in vitro fertilization (IVF) is transferred to the uterus of a surrogate — also called a gestational carrier. The embryo may be created using the intended mother's eggs and the intended father's sperm, or with donor eggs, donor sperm, or both. Critically, the surrogate has no genetic connection to the baby she carries. She is not the biological mother.

This is the defining difference from traditional surrogacy, in which the surrogate's own egg is used — making her the genetic mother. Traditional surrogacy is rarely practiced in the United States today due to the significant legal and emotional risks that come with that genetic connection.

💡 Over 95% of surrogacy arrangements in the United States today are gestational — meaning the surrogate has no biological relationship to the child she carries.

Who Pursues Gestational Surrogacy?

Intended parents are a diverse group. The most common reasons people pursue gestational surrogacy include:

  • Medical conditions affecting the uterus: Including uterine absence (MRKH syndrome), significant uterine fibroids, Asherman's syndrome, prior hysterectomy, or a uterus that cannot safely support a pregnancy.
  • Serious health conditions: Heart disease, kidney disease, lupus, cancer history, or other conditions where pregnancy would pose significant risk.
  • Recurrent implantation failure: When embryo quality is good but implantation repeatedly fails despite multiple IVF cycles.
  • Recurrent pregnancy loss: Multiple miscarriages, particularly in the second trimester.
  • Same-sex male couples: Two male partners need both an egg donor and a gestational surrogate to have a biological child.
  • Single men: Single men pursue surrogacy similarly — requiring an egg donor and a surrogate.
  • Transgender individuals: Trans women and non-binary individuals who have undergone gender-affirming surgery affecting reproductive organs.
  • Single women: Women who cannot safely carry a pregnancy may use their own eggs and a surrogate.

What Is a Gestational Carrier Agreement (GCA)?

Before any medical procedures begin, intended parents and their surrogate must sign a Gestational Carrier Agreement — a detailed legal contract negotiated by separate, independent attorneys for each party. This document covers compensation, expense reimbursements, medical decision-making, number of embryos to transfer, protocols for selective reduction, communication expectations, and how parental rights will be legally established.

💡 No reputable agency or attorney will allow medical steps to begin before the GCA is fully executed.

Gestational vs. Traditional Surrogacy

Gestational surrogacy: Surrogate has no genetic connection to the baby. Embryo created via IVF. The standard in the US. Cleaner legal protections for all parties.

Traditional surrogacy: Surrogate is also the egg donor and genetic mother. Creates significant legal complexity around parental rights. Most US agencies, fertility clinics, and attorneys no longer facilitate traditional surrogacy.

Is Gestational Surrogacy Legal?

Gestational surrogacy is legal in most US states, but laws vary significantly. Some states have comprehensive statutes, some rely on case law, and a few restrict or prohibit commercial surrogacy entirely. The state where your surrogate delivers determines your legal protections. See our complete state-by-state legal guide.

How Long Does Gestational Surrogacy Take?

A typical journey takes 12 to 18 months from starting your agency search to the birth of your child. This includes: agency selection (1–2 months), surrogate matching (1–4 months), legal contracts (3–6 weeks), medical screening and IVF (2–3 months), and the pregnancy (approximately 9 months).
