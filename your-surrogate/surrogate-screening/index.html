How Surrogates Are Screened: A Guide for Intended Parents

Before you're matched with a surrogate, she has passed rigorous screening. Here is what that process looks like — and why each step matters.

Background Check

A comprehensive criminal background check is standard for all surrogates and, usually, all adults living in the surrogate's home. This covers criminal history, sex offender registries, and financial/identity verification. No agency should allow a surrogate to proceed without a clean background check.

Medical History Review

Surrogates are required to share complete obstetric and general medical records. The agency and fertility clinic review prior pregnancies (ideally at least one successful, complication-free delivery), any history of pregnancy complications, overall health status, BMI, medications, smoking, and substance use history.

Psychological Evaluation

A licensed mental health professional with experience in third-party reproduction evaluates the surrogate (and typically her partner, if she has one) to assess emotional readiness for surrogacy, understanding of the process, realistic expectations about the relationship with intended parents, and any risk factors. The intended parents also undergo a psychological evaluation.

💡 A psychological evaluation that raises concerns is not necessarily a disqualifier — but it may lead to recommendations for pre-journey counseling or additional support. Take these assessments seriously; they protect everyone.

Fertility Clinic Medical Screening

After matching, the surrogate undergoes a full medical evaluation at your fertility clinic: uterine cavity evaluation (to confirm no polyps, fibroids, or structural issues), infectious disease testing (FDA required for all third-party reproductive arrangements), complete blood panel, and review of her full OB history. The clinic issues a formal medical clearance letter before any legal or financial processes advance.

Home Study (Some Agencies)

Some agencies conduct a home visit or home study to confirm the surrogate's home environment is stable and safe. Not all agencies require this, but it is an additional layer of screening that some intended parents value.
