    Surrogate Screening: 5 Layers of Vetting Explained

    What goes into surrogate screening — agency pre-screen, background check, psychological evaluation, medical history review, and fertility clinic medical clearance.

    Layer 1: Agency Pre-Screen

    The first screen happens when a woman applies to an agency’s surrogate program. The agency reviews her basic eligibility: age, BMI, prior pregnancy history, health status, living situation, and absence of government financial assistance. Most applicants are screened out at this stage before ever speaking with a case manager.

    Layer 2: Background Check

    Criminal background checks (federal and state) and sex offender registry checks are run on the surrogate and typically all adults in her household. Drug screens are conducted. Financial background may also be reviewed. Felony convictions, drug use, or household members with criminal records are typically disqualifying.

    Layer 3: Psychological Evaluation

    A licensed mental health professional who specializes in third-party reproduction conducts a psychological evaluation of the surrogate (and often her partner, if applicable). The evaluation assesses: genuine understanding of the arrangement, emotional readiness to carry a pregnancy for someone else, ability to relinquish the baby at birth, history of mental health issues, motivations for surrogacy, and understanding of what the intended parents’ role will be. The written report becomes part of the surrogate’s file and is reviewed by the fertility clinic.

    Layer 4: Medical Records Review

    The agency and/or the fertility clinic reviews the surrogate’s obstetric records for all prior pregnancies — looking for complications, C-sections, placental issues, preterm birth, and other factors that might affect her ability to safely carry another pregnancy.

    Layer 5: Fertility Clinic Medical Evaluation

    The final and most rigorous screen. The fertility clinic (where the embryo transfer will take place) conducts a full medical evaluation: uterine assessment via saline sonogram or hysteroscopy, full blood panel including hormone levels, infectious disease screening (HIV, hepatitis, STIs), and review of all prior records. The clinic has final say on medical clearance. A surrogate who passes all agency screens may still be declined at the clinic level.

    The 5-layer screening process exists to protect intended parents, surrogates, and the children born through these arrangements. Understand each layer before your surrogate reaches the clinic — so you know what to expect and what could cause delay.
