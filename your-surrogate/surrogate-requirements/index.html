    Surrogate Requirements in the US: Age, Health & Eligibility

    Standard requirements for gestational surrogates in the US — age, prior pregnancy history, BMI, health history, lifestyle, and medical disqualifiers.

    ASRM Guidelines: The Clinical Standard

    The American Society for Reproductive Medicine (ASRM) publishes guidelines for gestational carrier eligibility that most fertility clinics follow. These are not law but are the clinical standard that governs what the fertility clinic will accept for embryo transfer. A surrogate can pass an agency screen but still be declined by the fertility clinic at the medical evaluation stage.

    Standard Requirements

    • Age: Most agencies and fertility clinics require gestational surrogates to be between 21 and 40 years old (some clinics cap at 38). ASRM guidelines recommend not using surrogates over 45.
    • Prior successful pregnancy: Almost universally required. The surrogate must have carried at least one pregnancy to term and delivered a healthy baby. This demonstrates she can achieve and maintain a pregnancy.
    • BMI: Most fertility clinics require a BMI under 32–35 (the specific cutoff varies). Higher BMI can increase pregnancy risks and affect uterine lining response.
    • Stable mental health: No active treatment for significant mental health conditions. Psychological evaluation is required to assess motivation, emotional readiness, and understanding of the arrangement.
    • Non-smoker: No tobacco use for at least 12 months. Some clinics also screen for nicotine via urine test.
    • No illicit drug use: Screened via drug panel. Marijuana use disqualifies candidates even in states where recreational use is legal.
    • No recent antidepressant/psychiatric medication changes: Stable mental health history; no recent medication changes.
    • Financial stability: Must not be receiving government financial assistance based on income (TANF/welfare). Required to ensure compensation is not the sole motivation.
    • Completed family: Some agencies and clinics prefer surrogates who are not planning to have more children of their own.

    Medical Disqualifiers

    Conditions that typically disqualify a surrogate candidate include: prior uterine surgeries that affect uterine integrity, Asherman’s syndrome, uterine fibroids that affect the cavity, uncontrolled diabetes or hypertension, autoimmune conditions affecting pregnancy, history of preterm birth or placental complications, and HIV or certain other infectious diseases. The fertility clinic makes the final determination after the full medical evaluation.

    Your agency screens surrogates before presenting them; the fertility clinic screens them again before the transfer. Two layers of medical vetting protect intended parents and surrogates alike.
