Surrogate Requirements: What Intended Parents Should Know

Understanding what qualifies a woman to be a surrogate helps intended parents evaluate candidates confidently and set realistic expectations for the screening process.

Standard Surrogate Qualifications

While specific requirements vary by agency and fertility clinic, the standard qualifications for gestational surrogates in the United States include:

  • Age: Typically 21–40, though most agencies prefer 23–38
  • Prior pregnancy: At least one successful, uncomplicated pregnancy and delivery, with the child currently in the surrogate's custody
  • BMI: Most clinics require a BMI under 32–35
  • Non-smoker: No tobacco use, typically for at least 12 months
  • No illicit drug use: Clean drug screening required
  • No significant criminal history
  • Financially stable: Not receiving government assistance (in most agency guidelines)
  • Psychologically stable: Cleared by a licensed mental health professional
  • Supportive home environment
  • US citizen or legal permanent resident (for most domestic arrangements)

Medical Disqualifiers

Even a candidate who meets all agency criteria may be disqualified during the fertility clinic's medical screening. Common medical disqualifiers include: uterine abnormalities (fibroids, polyps, septum), certain infectious diseases, history of preterm delivery or preeclampsia, certain medications incompatible with pregnancy, or other conditions that increase obstetric risk.

💡 It's not uncommon for 10–20% of agency-screened surrogates to be disqualified at the fertility clinic medical screening stage. This is normal and is why clinics require their own evaluation regardless of agency screening.

Why These Requirements Protect Everyone

These requirements exist to protect the surrogate's health and safety, maximize the probability of a successful pregnancy, and reduce legal and ethical risk for intended parents. Agencies and clinics that screen rigorously are the ones that consistently achieve the best outcomes.
