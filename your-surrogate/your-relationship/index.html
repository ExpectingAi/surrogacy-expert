    Your Relationship with Your Surrogate: Building a Healthy Partnership

    How to build a respectful, healthy relationship with your gestational surrogate — communication expectations, navigating differences, the emotional reality for both parties, and what comes after birth.

    Setting Expectations Before You Match

    The most important relationship work happens before the match is finalized. During your first meeting with a potential surrogate, discuss openly: how often you want to communicate, which communication channels you prefer, whether you want to attend prenatal appointments, your preferences around being present at the birth, and what ongoing contact, if any, you envision after the baby is born. Misaligned expectations are the most common source of friction in surrogacy relationships — the best time to surface them is before you match, not after.

    Communication During the Pregnancy

    Most intended parents and surrogates communicate frequently — texting after appointments, sharing ultrasound photos, regular video calls. The right frequency depends on what both parties agree to. What matters most is consistency: check in regularly, respond promptly, and be present in the ways you agreed to be.

    Respecting Her Autonomy

    Your surrogate has complete authority over medical decisions during the pregnancy. She is the patient; her body, her doctors, her choices. The GCA documents agreements on key issues — number of embryos transferred, selective reduction protocols — but her day-to-day medical decisions during the pregnancy are hers. Respecting this boundary is both legally required and essential to the trust that makes the relationship work.

    When Friction Arises

    Friction in surrogacy relationships is normal. Common sources include: different communication frequencies than expected, decisions during the pregnancy that weren’t explicitly anticipated, anxiety on both sides during difficult moments, and different expectations about the birth. Use your agency’s case manager as a mediator when needed — that’s part of what you’re paying for.

    After the Birth

    What happens after the birth is whatever you’ve both agreed to. Some intended parents and surrogates maintain a close, ongoing friendship. Others exchange periodic updates and photos. Some prefer a clean close after the journey is complete. All of these are valid — what matters is that both parties know what to expect and that neither feels pressure to maintain a level of contact that doesn’t feel natural.

    The relationship with your surrogate is unlike any other in your life. It works best when both parties approach it with genuine respect, clear communication, and realistic expectations from the start.
