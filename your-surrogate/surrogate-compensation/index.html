Surrogate Compensation: What Intended Parents Pay and Why

Surrogate compensation is the largest single cost in most surrogacy journeys. Here is exactly what it covers and why it is structured the way it is.

Base Compensation

A surrogate's base fee — paid in monthly installments over the course of the pregnancy — typically ranges from $30,000 to $60,000. The amount depends on: prior surrogacy experience (experienced surrogates command higher fees), state of residence (California and other high-cost states are typically higher), and agency-specific compensation guidelines.

Additional Compensation Items

Beyond base compensation, surrogates typically receive:

  • Embryo transfer fee: $750–$1,500 per transfer attempt
  • Monthly allowance: $200–$300/month for incidental expenses during the pregnancy
  • Maternity clothing: ~$750 lump sum
  • Travel reimbursement: Mileage or flights for all medical appointments
  • Childcare reimbursement: For the surrogate's children during medical appointments
  • Lost wages: If the surrogate misses work for medically required bed rest or appointments
  • Housekeeping: If bed rest is required (typically $250–$300/week)
  • C-section fee: Additional $2,500–$4,000 if a cesarean is required
  • Multiples fee: Additional $5,000–$10,000 if twins or triplets are confirmed
  • Life insurance: $250K–$500K term policy, premiums paid by intended parents
  • Selective reduction fee: If applicable and agreed upon in the GCA

💡 Surrogate compensation in the US is fully legal. Surrogates are compensated for their extraordinary commitment, time, physical risk, and the life-changing gift they provide. Fair compensation attracts experienced, committed surrogates.

How Escrow Works

All surrogate compensation and expense funds are held in a neutral third-party escrow account, usually managed by a specialty company experienced in surrogacy arrangements. Intended parents fund the escrow before the legal process begins, and disbursements follow the schedule outlined in the GCA. This protects both parties: surrogates know the funds are secured; intended parents maintain accountability over how they're released.
