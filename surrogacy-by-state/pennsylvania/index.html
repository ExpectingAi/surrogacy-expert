    Surrogacy Laws in Pennsylvania: Guide for Intended Parents

    Pennsylvania is a moderate surrogacy state with no dedicated statute, governed by case law and general parentage principles. Pre-birth orders are available in many counties, and neighboring New Jersey — one of the most surrogacy-friendly states in the country — is often a practical delivery alternative for Philadelphia-area surrogates.

    Legal Framework

    Pennsylvania has no specific surrogacy statute. Gestational surrogacy is governed by case law, the Pennsylvania Domestic Relations Code, and general contract principles. Pennsylvania courts have generally recognized gestational carrier agreements and issued parentage orders for intended parents, but the process and outcomes vary significantly by county. Philadelphia and its suburbs have a well-developed surrogacy legal practice; rural Pennsylvania courts may have little to no experience with surrogacy cases.

    Pre-Birth Orders in Pennsylvania

    Pre-birth orders are available in Pennsylvania but are not uniformly granted across all counties. Philadelphia County, Montgomery County, Delaware County, and Allegheny County (Pittsburgh) have more experience with surrogacy cases and are more likely to issue PBOs. Courts in more rural or conservative counties may decline PBOs or impose additional requirements. When a PBO is not available, a post-birth parentage order is required — a process that takes several weeks and during which intended parents should carry documentation of their legal status when traveling.

    Pennsylvania vs. New Jersey: The Key Comparison

    For many Philadelphia-area surrogates, delivering across the state line in New Jersey can provide significantly stronger legal protections. New Jersey enacted the New Jersey Gestational Carrier Agreement Act in 2018, providing explicit statutory authorization for surrogacy agreements and routine pre-birth orders for all family types including LGBTQ+ parents. If your surrogate lives in the Philadelphia suburbs, it is worth discussing with your attorney whether a New Jersey hospital delivery is logistically feasible. Many intended parents in the Delaware Valley pursue exactly this strategy.

    LGBTQ+ Intended Parents

    Pennsylvania does not have explicit statutory protections for LGBTQ+ families in surrogacy contexts. Same-sex couples and single LGBTQ+ intended parents have obtained parentage orders in Pennsylvania, but outcomes depend heavily on the county and presiding judge. Philadelphia County is the most reliably favorable jurisdiction for LGBTQ+ intended parents within Pennsylvania. For the most predictable outcome, LGBTQ+ families with Philadelphia-area surrogates should seriously evaluate New Jersey delivery.

    Pennsylvania's Surrogacy Community

    Pennsylvania has a substantial and active surrogate community, particularly in the Philadelphia metro, Pittsburgh, and Lehigh Valley areas. The state has strong fertility clinic infrastructure, with multiple well-regarded reproductive endocrinology practices. Surrogate compensation rates in Pennsylvania tend to be moderate compared to California or New York, making Pennsylvania surrogacy journeys potentially more cost-effective for intended parents already connected to a Pennsylvania surrogate.

    Key Steps for Pennsylvania Intended Parents

    Engage a Pennsylvania-licensed reproductive attorney early — ideally before finalizing your surrogate match — so they can advise on county selection and whether a cross-border New Jersey delivery makes sense in your specific situation. Your attorney will draft or review the Gestational Carrier Agreement, coordinate legal clearance with the fertility clinic, and file for parentage. Both intended parents and the surrogate must have independent legal representation.

    Pennsylvania has a strong surrogacy community and good fertility infrastructure. For Philadelphia-area surrogates, New Jersey delivery under the 2018 GCAA is often the most legally straightforward option.
