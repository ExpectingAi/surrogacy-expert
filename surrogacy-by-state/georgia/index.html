    Surrogacy Laws in Georgia: Guide for Intended Parents

    Georgia is a moderate surrogacy state with no dedicated statute, governed by case law. Pre-birth orders are available in many counties, particularly in the Atlanta metro area. Georgia has a large and active surrogacy community, making it one of the most commonly used moderate-complexity states in the Southeast.

    Legal Framework

    Georgia has no surrogacy-specific statute. Gestational surrogacy is governed by case law and Georgia's general parentage framework under the Georgia Domestic Relations Code. Georgia courts have generally been willing to recognize gestational carrier agreements and issue parentage orders, but outcomes are not uniform across the state. Judges in the Atlanta metro area are considerably more experienced with surrogacy than those in rural Georgia, and county selection matters a great deal.

    Pre-Birth Orders in Georgia

    Pre-birth orders are available in Georgia in many counties, particularly in the metro Atlanta area. Fulton County, Gwinnett County, Cobb County, DeKalb County, and Cherokee County have courts experienced with surrogacy cases. Courts in more rural areas of Georgia may have limited surrogacy experience and may decline to issue PBOs or impose additional procedural requirements. When a PBO cannot be obtained, a post-birth parentage order is required before the birth certificate can be corrected.

    Your Georgia-licensed reproductive attorney will assess which county your surrogate is delivering in and advise on the expected process. In some cases, if your surrogate lives near the Atlanta metro but is delivering in a less experienced county, your attorney may advise on the logistics of delivering at a hospital within a more favorable jurisdiction.

    LGBTQ+ Intended Parents

    Georgia does not have explicit statutory protections for LGBTQ+ families in surrogacy. Same-sex couples and single LGBTQ+ intended parents have obtained parentage orders in Georgia courts, particularly in Fulton County (Atlanta), but outcomes vary by judge. Georgia does not have a constitutional or statutory prohibition on LGBTQ+ parentage, and many Atlanta-area judges have handled same-sex couple surrogacy cases without difficulty. However, LGBTQ+ intended parents should discuss realistic expectations with their attorney before choosing Georgia as a delivery state.

    Georgia's Surrogacy Community

    Despite the moderate legal complexity, Georgia has one of the most active surrogacy communities in the Southeast. Atlanta is home to multiple well-regarded fertility clinics and reproductive law practices with deep surrogacy experience. The cost of living in Georgia means surrogate compensation rates tend to be somewhat lower than in coastal markets like California or New York — intended parents working with a Georgia surrogate often find the overall journey cost more manageable. Many national agencies actively recruit surrogates in Georgia for exactly this reason.

    Key Steps for Georgia Intended Parents

    Before finalizing your match with a Georgia surrogate, retain a Georgia-licensed reproductive attorney who can assess the county, advise on county selection if relevant, and confirm the expected legal pathway. Your attorney will draft or review the Gestational Carrier Agreement, coordinate legal clearance with your fertility clinic, and manage the parentage order process. Both parties must have independent legal representation.

    Georgia has a strong Atlanta-area surrogacy ecosystem with experienced courts, good fertility infrastructure, and a large surrogate community. County selection is the key variable — work with an experienced local attorney from the start.
