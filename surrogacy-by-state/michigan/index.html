    Surrogacy Laws in Michigan: Guide for Intended Parents

    Michigan presents unique legal challenges for surrogacy: under MCL 722.855, surrogacy contracts are void and unenforceable. This does not mean surrogacy is impossible in Michigan, but it requires careful legal navigation.

    Legal Framework

    Michigan Compiled Laws §722.855 explicitly states that surrogacy contracts are void and unenforceable. Critically, the statute also provides for criminal penalties for those who participate in or assist with compensated surrogacy arrangements. Michigan is one of the very few states where surrogacy agreements carry potential criminal liability for the parties and their attorneys.

    What Does This Mean for Intended Parents?

    If your potential surrogate lives in Michigan, the GCA itself would be unenforceable under Michigan law. This does not mean a pregnancy cannot occur, but it means: neither party has a legally binding contract, intended parents cannot enforce the agreement if the surrogate changes her mind, the surrogate cannot enforce compensation terms if intended parents default, and parental rights must be established through post-birth proceedings.

    Can Surrogacy Still Work in Michigan?

    Some intended parents do pursue surrogacy with Michigan-based surrogates, relying entirely on trust rather than legal enforcement. However, this approach carries significant legal and financial risk. Most surrogacy professionals strongly advise against using Michigan as a delivery state, or matching with a Michigan-based surrogate, unless she is willing to deliver in a neighboring surrogacy-friendly state.

    ⚠️ Michigan is one of the highest-risk surrogacy states in the US. Consult with both a Michigan-licensed and a surrogacy-friendly-state reproductive attorney before proceeding with any arrangement involving a Michigan surrogate.

    Alternative Options

    Michigan-based intended parents commonly pursue surrogates in neighboring states with strong surrogacy frameworks: Illinois (GSA 2005), Ohio (moderate), or Indiana (moderate). A willing Michigan surrogate may also agree to deliver in Illinois or another nearby surrogacy-friendly state.
