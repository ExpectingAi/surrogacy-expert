    Surrogacy Laws in Michigan: Guide for Intended Parents

    Michigan has one of the most restrictive surrogacy legal frameworks in the US. The 1988 Surrogate Parenting Act makes compensated surrogacy contracts void and unenforceable, and criminalizes entering into or assisting with a compensated surrogacy arrangement. Michigan surrogates frequently deliver in neighboring Illinois or Ohio.

    Michigan's 1988 Surrogate Parenting Act

    Michigan Compiled Laws § 722.851–722.863 (the Surrogate Parenting Act) declares compensated surrogate parenting contracts void and unenforceable as contrary to public policy. Beyond voiding contracts, the SPA also imposes criminal penalties on anyone who enters into, induces, arranges, procures, or otherwise assists in a surrogacy arrangement for compensation. This places Michigan among the most restrictive US jurisdictions for surrogacy.

    Uncompensated (altruistic) surrogacy exists in a legal gray zone in Michigan — the SPA specifically targets compensated arrangements. Some altruistic arrangements have proceeded in Michigan with court oversight, but the legal risk and complexity is substantial even in those cases.

    What Michigan Surrogates Should Do

    Michigan surrogates almost universally deliver outside Michigan. Illinois is the most common choice — the Illinois Gestational Surrogacy Act (2005) provides a comprehensive statutory framework, routine pre-birth orders for all family types, and strong legal protections for all parties. Illinois borders Michigan's southern Lower Peninsula, and delivering in the Chicago area or other Illinois cities is logistically feasible for many Michigan surrogates.

    Ohio is the other common option, particularly for Michigan surrogates in the southeastern part of the state near Toledo and Cleveland.

    LGBTQ+ Intended Parents

    Given that surrogacy in Michigan is already legally untenable under the SPA, LGBTQ+ families with Michigan surrogates should focus on cross-border delivery rather than attempting any Michigan-based legal process.

    Key Steps

    If your surrogate is in Michigan, begin by retaining attorneys licensed in both Michigan and your chosen delivery state (Illinois or Ohio). The delivery state's law governs your parentage order. Do not attempt to process a surrogacy arrangement under Michigan law — consult an experienced reproductive attorney immediately.

    Do not deliver in Michigan under a compensated surrogacy arrangement. Illinois delivery under the 2005 GSA is the standard solution for Michigan surrogates — experienced Michigan surrogacy attorneys coordinate this routinely.
