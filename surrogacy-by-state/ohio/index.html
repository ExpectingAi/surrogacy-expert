    Surrogacy Laws in Ohio: Guide for Intended Parents

    Ohio is a moderate surrogacy state governed by case law rather than statute. Pre-birth orders are available in many counties, and Ohio has a large surrogate population — but legal outcomes depend heavily on which county your surrogate delivers in.

    Legal Framework

    Ohio has no comprehensive surrogacy statute. Gestational surrogacy arrangements are governed by case law, general contract principles, and Ohio Revised Code provisions related to parentage. Ohio courts have generally upheld gestational carrier agreements and issued parentage orders for intended parents, but outcomes are not uniform across all 88 counties. The strength of your legal position depends significantly on where your surrogate delivers.

    Pre-Birth Orders in Ohio

    Pre-birth orders (PBOs) are available in Ohio but are not uniform statewide. Courts in Franklin County (Columbus), Hamilton County (Cincinnati), Cuyahoga County (Cleveland), and Summit County (Akron) have more experience with surrogacy cases and are more likely to issue PBOs routinely. Courts in rural counties may have little to no surrogacy experience, and some judges may decline to issue PBOs or require post-birth proceedings instead.

    When a PBO is granted, intended parents are named on the birth certificate at delivery without the surrogate's name appearing. When a PBO is not available, a post-birth parentage order must be obtained — a court proceeding that takes several weeks to a few months after birth. During this period, intended parents should carry documentation of their legal status when traveling with the baby.

    Choosing a Delivery County

    One of the most important decisions for Ohio surrogacy journeys is where your surrogate will deliver. If your surrogate lives in a rural county with an inexperienced court, your attorney may advise having her deliver at a hospital in a more surrogacy-friendly metro county — particularly if she lives near Columbus, Cincinnati, Cleveland, or Akron. Discuss county selection with your attorney before finalizing your surrogate match.

    LGBTQ+ Intended Parents

    Ohio does not have explicit statutory protections for LGBTQ+ families in surrogacy, and the legal environment is more variable than in surrogacy-friendly states. Same-sex couples and single LGBTQ+ intended parents have successfully obtained parentage orders in Ohio, but outcomes depend on the county and judge. Franklin County (Columbus) is generally the most favorable for LGBTQ+ intended parents. Working with an attorney who has specific experience obtaining LGBTQ+ parentage orders in Ohio courts is essential.

    Ohio's Surrogacy Infrastructure

    Ohio has significant advantages for intended parents beyond its legal framework. The state has a large and active surrogate community, particularly in the Columbus, Cincinnati, and Cleveland metro areas. Multiple well-regarded fertility clinics operate in Ohio, including practices affiliated with major academic medical centers. Agency fees and surrogate compensation in Ohio tend to be somewhat lower than in California or New York markets, which can make Ohio journeys more cost-effective for intended parents whose surrogates are already located there.

    Key Steps for Ohio Intended Parents

    Retain an Ohio-licensed reproductive attorney before finalizing your surrogate match — ideally before you even begin agency intake, so the attorney can advise on county selection. Once matched, your attorney will draft or review the Gestational Carrier Agreement, coordinate with the fertility clinic on legal clearance, and file for a pre-birth or post-birth parentage order depending on your delivery county. Both parties must be represented by independent counsel.

    Ohio's large surrogate community and strong fertility infrastructure make it a practical option for many intended parents — but county selection is critical. Retain an experienced reproductive attorney before finalizing your match.
