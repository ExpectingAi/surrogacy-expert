    Surrogacy Laws by State: All 50 States + DC

    Where your surrogate delivers determines your legal protections — which parentage order you can get, how fast, and whether LGBTQ+ families have explicit statutory protection. Use this guide to understand your state before choosing a surrogate.

    Why the delivery state matters

    Surrogacy law is state law — not federal. The state where your surrogate delivers determines: (1) whether a pre-birth order is available, (2) how quickly your names appear on the birth certificate, and (3) whether same-sex couples and single parents have explicit statutory protection. In some cases, choosing a delivery hospital just across a state line can dramatically improve your legal position. See our legal process guide for how parentage orders work.

    ✅ Surrogacy-Friendly States

    These states have comprehensive surrogacy statutes or strong, predictable case law. Pre-birth orders are routine for all intended parent configurations including LGBTQ+ families and single parents.

    ★ California ★ Nevada Washington Oregon Colorado ★ Illinois ★ New York New Jersey Connecticut Maine New Hampshire Rhode Island Vermont Delaware Maryland Virginia New Mexico Hawaii Massachusetts Washington DC

    ⚠️ Moderate States

    Case law governs in these states. Pre-birth orders are available in major metro courts but county selection matters. Intended parents should retain experienced local counsel before finalizing a match.

    Texas Florida Georgia Ohio Pennsylvania Minnesota Arizona North Carolina Indiana Missouri Tennessee Utah Kentucky Iowa Kansas Nebraska Oklahoma Arkansas Alabama Idaho Montana Wyoming Alaska West Virginia

    ⚡ Complex States

    These states have hostile statutes or near-zero court experience with surrogacy. Cross-border delivery to a neighboring friendly or moderate state is strongly recommended.

    Michigan Wisconsin North Dakota South Dakota South Carolina Mississippi

    🚫 Restricted States

    Commercial surrogacy contracts are prohibited or void by statute. Do not attempt surrogacy delivery in these states.

    Louisiana
