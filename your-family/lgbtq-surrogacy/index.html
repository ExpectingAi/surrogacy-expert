LGBTQ+ Surrogacy: Building Your Family as an LGBTQ+ Intended Parent

Surrogacy is one of the most well-established paths to parenthood for LGBTQ+ individuals and couples. Here is everything you need to know.

Same-Sex Male Couples

For same-sex male couples, gestational surrogacy combined with egg donation is the primary path to having a biological child. Both partners have the option to use their own sperm — either selecting one for a given cycle, or fertilizing eggs with both and transferring one embryo from each partner. The fertility clinic will walk you through your genetic options.

Legally, in surrogacy-friendly states, both partners can be named on the birth certificate through a pre-birth order, regardless of which partner's sperm was used. In some states, the non-genetic parent may need to complete a second-parent adoption to secure equal legal standing.

Same-Sex Female Couples

Same-sex female couples pursuing surrogacy typically do so because neither partner can carry a pregnancy. This may be due to medical conditions, prior hysterectomy, or other health factors. In some arrangements, one partner provides eggs and the other's embryo is carried by the surrogate — creating a biological connection for both partners. This is sometimes called "reciprocal IVF."

Transgender Intended Parents

Transgender individuals who have undergone gender-affirming surgery affecting reproductive organs may pursue surrogacy as their path to biological parenthood. Consult with a reproductive endocrinologist early — ideally before or during gender transition — to understand fertility preservation and family-building options.

💡 Choose a surrogacy agency and fertility clinic with demonstrated experience working with LGBTQ+ intended parents. Look for explicit inclusivity statements, staff training, and confirmed experience with same-sex legal arrangements in your target state.

Choosing the Right State

LGBTQ+ intended parents should prioritize surrogacy-friendly states with explicit legal protections for same-sex parents: California, Nevada, Washington, Connecticut, Maine, New Hampshire, Rhode Island, New Jersey, Illinois, and New York are among the strongest. Avoid states with restrictive surrogacy statutes or limited recognition of same-sex parental rights.

Legal Protections

Work with a reproductive attorney experienced in LGBTQ+ family building. The Obergefell v. Hodges (2015) Supreme Court decision established equal marriage rights, which provides a foundation — but surrogacy law operates at the state level and varies significantly. Your attorney will ensure both partners are fully protected as legal parents from day one.
