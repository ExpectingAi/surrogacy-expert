    Surrogacy for International Intended Parents

    The US is the world’s top surrogacy destination for international intended parents. Here’s what you need to know about navigating two legal systems, choosing the right delivery state, and getting home with your child.

    Why International Intended Parents Choose the US

    The United States has become the world’s leading surrogacy destination for international families. India, Thailand, Cambodia, and Nepal have all closed to commercial surrogacy. Ukraine has become inaccessible due to ongoing conflict. The US offers what most other countries cannot: enforceable contracts, pre-birth parentage orders in surrogacy-friendly states, world-class fertility clinics, and internationally recognized birth certificates. The cost is higher than alternatives that once existed — most journeys run $150,000–$220,000 in 2026 — but the legal certainty and documentation quality are unmatched.

    The Two Legal Systems You Must Navigate

    Every international surrogacy journey involves two separate legal systems operating in parallel:

    • US law (delivery state): Establishes legal parentage under US law via a pre-birth order. This is the foundation — your US birth certificate and parentage order are what everything else builds on.
    • Home country law: Determines how your home country recognizes the US documentation, and what citizenship, nationality, or residency rights your child has. This is entirely separate from US law and must be managed with a family law attorney in your home country.

    Start both attorney relationships before your child is born. Some home-country processes take months and cannot be rushed after the fact.

    Best Delivery States for International Families

    California is the most internationally recognized delivery state — California birth certificates and parentage orders are familiar to consulates and foreign government offices worldwide. Nevada, New York, and Illinois are also well-recognized. For international families, the strength of the US legal foundation matters more than cost savings — any ambiguity in US documents creates problems downstream in your home country.

    Practical Logistics

    • Arrive early. Plan to be in the US 3–4 weeks before the due date. Missing the birth is a real risk for international parents who cut timing too close.
    • US address. You need a US address for legal correspondence. A trusted contact, attorney’s office, or mail forwarding service works.
    • Travel documents. Your child needs travel documents to leave the US. Apply for a passport from your home country using the US birth certificate. This typically takes 2–6 weeks depending on your country’s consulate.
    • Visa. A B-1/B-2 tourist visa is most common. Ensure yours is valid for the full expected stay — plan for 4–6 weeks of US residence after the birth.
    • Bank/escrow. You need wire transfer capability to fund escrow before the embryo transfer. Discuss this with your escrow company early.

Can you connect me with references from past international clients? Do you have experience with my country’s specific documentation requirements?
