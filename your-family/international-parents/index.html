    International Surrogacy in the US: Guide for International Intended Parents

    Why international intended parents choose the United States for surrogacy — two legal systems to navigate, citizenship and passport logistics, and what to plan for before the birth.

    Why International Intended Parents Choose the US

    The United States is the world's most popular destination for international intended parents pursuing gestational surrogacy. The reasons are significant: strong and predictable legal protections in surrogacy-friendly states, high IVF success rates at leading fertility clinics, a large and well-screened surrogate community, and the ability to obtain a pre-birth order that names intended parents from day one. Many countries that once had accessible surrogacy programs (India, Thailand, Nepal, Cambodia) have closed or heavily restricted commercial surrogacy, making the US the most legally reliable option globally.

    The Two Legal Systems

    International intended parents must navigate two entirely separate legal systems: US law (establishing parentage in the delivery state) and their home country's law (recognizing those parental rights and conferring citizenship or residency on the child). These are independent processes that must both be addressed.

    On the US side, working with a surrogacy-friendly delivery state — California, Nevada, New York, Illinois, New Jersey, Washington — provides the strongest and most internationally recognized parentage documentation. The US birth certificate and pre-birth order form the foundation of the child's legal identity for home-country proceedings.

    Home Country Recognition

    Every country has its own rules for recognizing US-issued parentage documents and conferring citizenship. Some countries (Israel, Canada, UK, Australia, many European countries) have established frameworks for surrogacy-born children. Others are more complex. You need a family law attorney in your home country who is familiar with surrogacy recognition — in addition to your US reproductive attorney — before your child is born. Do not leave home-country legal work until after the birth.

    Citizenship and Passports

    A child born in the US to at least one US-citizen intended parent may have a claim to US citizenship under certain conditions — consult a US immigration attorney if this applies to you. For non-US-citizen intended parents, the child is not automatically a US citizen by birth (jus soli applies to children born on US soil, but the child's legal parents are the intended parents, not the surrogate). Work with a US immigration attorney early to understand the child's travel document and visa situation before the birth.

    Practical Logistics for International Families

    Plan to be in the US for at least 4–6 weeks around the birth — ideally more — to allow time for the birth, hospital discharge, birth certificate issuance, and initial passport or travel document processing. Many international intended parents arrive in the US around 34–36 weeks of pregnancy. Establish a US address for correspondence, coordinate with your home-country attorney in advance, and ensure your escrow and legal team know you are based internationally.

    Agency Selection for International Parents

    Not all US surrogacy agencies have experience with international intended parents. Look for an agency that: has worked with intended parents from your country before, understands the two-system legal complexity, has established relationships with US reproductive attorneys experienced in international parentage, and can provide references from past international clients.

    International surrogacy in the US requires a US reproductive attorney and a home-country family law attorney working in parallel. Start both relationships early — ideally before you select an agency.
