    Single Parent Surrogacy: Complete Guide for Solo Intended Parents

    Everything single intended parents need to know about pursuing gestational surrogacy — whether you’re a single man or single woman, legal parenthood, practical planning, and building your support network.

    Single Men Pursuing Surrogacy

    Single men pursue surrogacy through the same process as same-sex male couples: both a gestational surrogate and an egg donor are needed. Single men use their own sperm (or donor sperm) to fertilize a donor’s eggs, creating embryos for transfer to the surrogate. Legally, the process is similar to same-sex male couples — choosing a surrogacy-friendly delivery state that explicitly covers single parents is important for a clean pre-birth order.

    Single Women Pursuing Surrogacy

    Single women typically pursue surrogacy because they cannot safely carry a pregnancy themselves. They may use their own eggs (fertilized with donor sperm) or donor eggs, depending on their medical situation. The legal process is generally simpler than for single men because the intended mother may be the genetic parent.

    Legal Parenthood for Single Parents

    In surrogacy-friendly states, a single intended parent can be named as the sole legal parent on the birth certificate via a pre-birth order. This is straightforward in states with comprehensive surrogacy statutes. In more restrictive states, single parents may face additional post-birth legal steps. California, Nevada, New York, Illinois, New Jersey, Washington, and Colorado all routinely handle single-parent PBOs.

    Practical Planning for Solo Parents

    Single parents should give extra thought to their support network before beginning the journey: who will be present at the birth, who provides backup care for the newborn while you attend medical appointments, and who you can count on in unexpected situations. Building this network in advance reduces stress during the journey and after the baby arrives.

    Financial Considerations

    Single parents carry the full financial cost of surrogacy without a co-parent to share it. Total costs typically run $80,000–$180,000. Building a realistic budget and securing financing before beginning the agency process is especially important for solo parents.

    Single parenthood through surrogacy is increasingly common. The legal process is well-established in the right states — what matters most is choosing the right team and building the right support network.
