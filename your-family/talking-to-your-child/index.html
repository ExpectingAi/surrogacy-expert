Talking to Your Child About Their Surrogacy Story

Research is clear: children who know their origin story from an early age integrate it as a natural part of their identity. Here is how to tell your surrogacy story with honesty and love.

The Research Consensus: Tell Early and Often

Psychologists and family therapists who specialize in donor-conceived and surrogate-born children consistently reach the same conclusion: children who know their origin story from infancy integrate it as a natural, normal part of who they are. Children who discover their surrogacy story as teenagers or adults frequently experience it as a shock — and sometimes as a betrayal of trust. There is no developmental downside to telling your child their story early.

Infancy and Toddlerhood (Ages 0–3)

Start now — even before your child can understand. The goal at this stage is normalizing the language in your household so there is never a singular "revelation" moment later. Simple, accurate language works perfectly: "You grew in another woman's belly. She was our surrogate, and she helped bring you to us. And now you're our family."

Early Childhood (Ages 3–7)

Children at this age begin asking concrete questions: "Why didn't I grow in your belly?" Answer directly and warmly. "My body wasn't able to grow a baby, so a kind woman named [surrogate's first name] carried you in her belly for us. She gave us the greatest gift anyone can give. And now you're ours." Children at this age are remarkably matter-of-fact about information delivered with matter-of-factness.

Middle Childhood (Ages 8–12)

Children at this age begin to understand more complex concepts: genetics, what pregnancy actually involves. They may ask about the surrogate: "Does she have kids? Does she miss me?" Answer honestly. If you have a relationship with your surrogate, share that. If you don't maintain regular contact, be honest about why without making it seem like the surrogate doesn't care.

Adolescence (Ages 13+)

Teenagers who have grown up knowing their story have usually integrated it comfortably. They may have deeper questions about genetics, the surrogate's motivations, or their sense of self. Create space for those conversations without anxiety or defensiveness. If your teenager expresses interest in meeting their surrogate, consider how to facilitate that thoughtfully.

Key Principles

  • Tell early — there is no "right age" to start. Start at birth.
  • Use accurate language — "surrogate," "gestational carrier," "embryo transfer" — not euphemisms
  • Make the story one of love, intention, and community — not hardship
  • The surrogate deserves to be spoken of with gratitude and respect — always
  • If you used donor genetic material, that story deserves the same openness
