    Same-Sex Male Couples: Complete Surrogacy Guide

    Everything same-sex male couples need to know about gestational surrogacy — egg donation, sperm decisions, split embryo strategies, delivery state selection, and how both fathers get on the birth certificate from day one.

    What Same-Sex Male Couples Need

    Two male partners pursuing gestational surrogacy need two things that heterosexual couples may not: an egg donor and a gestational surrogate. The egg donor and surrogate are almost always different people. Your sperm (from one or both partners) fertilizes the donor's eggs via IVF to create embryos, which are then transferred to your surrogate.

    Egg Donation

    Egg donors are found through your fertility clinic's internal donor database, a standalone egg donor agency, or as a known donor (a friend or family member who offers to donate). Anonymous donors are the most common choice. When selecting a donor, couples consider physical characteristics, education, health history, and personal statement. Egg donor fees typically range from $8,000–$15,000+ for compensation, plus clinic fees for the retrieval cycle.

    Whose Sperm? Split Embryos?

    Many same-sex male couples choose to split the retrieved eggs — fertilizing half with one partner's sperm and half with the other's. This creates two sets of embryos, each genetically related to one partner. The couple can then choose to transfer embryos from one partner first, or alternate. This approach allows both partners to potentially have a genetic connection to a child from the same surrogacy journey. Your fertility clinic and reproductive endocrinologist can advise on the logistics based on the donor's egg yield and your embryo situation.

    Both Fathers on the Birth Certificate

    In surrogacy-friendly states with comprehensive statutes — California, Nevada, New York, New Jersey, Illinois, Washington, Colorado, and others — both partners can be named on the birth certificate via a pre-birth order, regardless of which partner is the genetic father and regardless of whether they are legally married. This is one of the strongest arguments for choosing a surrogacy-friendly delivery state: both fathers are recognized as legal parents from the moment of birth, with no adoption required.

    Delivery State Matters

    Not all states offer equal protections for same-sex male couples. In surrogacy-friendly states, PBOs for same-sex couples are routine. In moderate-complexity states, the non-genetic partner may face additional legal steps or a second-parent adoption after the birth. Choosing the right delivery state is one of the most important legal decisions same-sex male couples make in the surrogacy process.
