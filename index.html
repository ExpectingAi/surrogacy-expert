Who pursues surrogacy

Is gestational surrogacy right for you?

People come to surrogacy for many different reasons. All of them are valid — here are the most common paths.

🏥

Medical necessity

Uterine conditions, prior hysterectomy, recurrent pregnancy loss, or health conditions that make carrying unsafe. The most common reason intended mothers pursue surrogacy.

🌈

LGBTQ+ family building

Same-sex male couples and transgender individuals most commonly build families through gestational surrogacy, often combined with egg donation.

👤

Single parenthood

Single men and women both pursue surrogacy. The legal and medical process is essentially the same as for couples.

🌍

International parents

The US is the world’s top surrogacy destination — strong legal framework, experienced professionals, and high IVF success rates.

💔

Recurrent pregnancy loss

When embryo quality is good but implantation repeatedly fails, a gestational surrogate’s uterus may provide the answer.

🧬

Egg donation + surrogacy

When you need both a donor and a surrogate, the two tracks run simultaneously — we explain exactly how the process works.

The process

From first steps to baby in your arms

A gestational surrogacy journey typically takes 12–18 months. Here is the shape of the road ahead.

1

Research & choose your agency

Understand the process, evaluate your budget, consult a reproductive attorney, and select the right agency. 1–2 months.

2

Match with a surrogate

Your agency presents surrogate profiles. You review, connect, and choose. Matching typically takes 1–4 months.

3

Legal contracts signed

Both parties retain independent attorneys and negotiate the Gestational Carrier Agreement. 3–6 weeks.

4

Medical & embryo transfer

Your surrogate passes medical screening at your fertility clinic, then undergoes the embryo transfer cycle.

5

Pregnancy, birth & your baby

Nine months of shared milestones, legal preparation, and anticipation — ending with your family.

Common questions

What intended parents ask most

In gestational surrogacy — the standard in the US today (95%+ of cases) — the surrogate carries an embryo created via IVF using the intended parents’ or donor’s genetic material. She has no genetic connection to the baby. In traditional surrogacy, the surrogate uses her own egg, making her the genetic mother. Traditional surrogacy carries significant legal and emotional risks and is rarely practiced in the US today.
Most intended parents find their surrogate through a professional surrogacy agency, which pre-screens candidates, manages the matching process, and coordinates communication. After reviewing surrogate profiles — including medical history, psychological evaluations, and personal statements — you choose who to meet. Matching typically takes 1–4 months.
Total costs typically range from $80,000 to $180,000, depending on surrogate compensation, agency fees, IVF costs, legal fees, and health insurance. California and major surrogacy markets tend toward the higher end. Our full cost guide breaks down every line item with low and high estimates.
Yes — in surrogacy-friendly states, intended parents are established as legal parents through a pre-birth order (PBO) issued before the baby is born. In some states a post-birth legal process is required. In gestational surrogacy, the surrogate has no legal claim to the child.
California, Nevada, Washington, Colorado, Illinois, Connecticut, New Jersey, New York, and Maine consistently rank as the most surrogacy-friendly states. They issue pre-birth orders for all family types and have well-established legal frameworks. See our state-by-state guide for all 50 states.
Yes. The United States is one of the world’s most popular surrogacy destinations for international intended parents. There is no residency requirement in any US state. California, Nevada, and Washington are the most popular states for international IPs. See our international parents guide.
