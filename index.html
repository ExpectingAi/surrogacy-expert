Who Surrogacy Is For

Is Gestational Surrogacy Right for You?

Surrogacy is a deeply personal decision. People pursue it for many reasons — and all of them are valid.

🏥

Medical Necessity

Conditions like uterine absence, repeated IVF failure, recurrent pregnancy loss, or health risks that make carrying a pregnancy unsafe are among the most common reasons intended parents pursue surrogacy.

🌈

LGBTQ+ Family Building

Same-sex male couples and transgender individuals regularly build their families through gestational surrogacy, often combined with egg donation. This is one of the most well-established paths to parenthood for LGBTQ+ families.

Single Parenthood

Single men and single women both pursue surrogacy as a path to parenthood. The process is the same in most jurisdictions — what changes is whether you'll also need an egg or sperm donor.

The Surrogacy Process

From First Steps to Baby in Your Arms

A gestational surrogacy journey typically takes 12–18 months from starting your agency search to birth. Here's the big picture.

1

Research & Agency

Understand your options, choose a surrogacy agency or go independent, and complete your application.

2

Match with a Surrogate

Your agency presents surrogate profiles. You review, connect, and choose. Matching takes weeks to a few months.

3

Legal Contracts

Both parties retain separate attorneys. The gestational carrier agreement protects everyone involved.

4

Medical & IVF

Your surrogate undergoes medical screening, then syncs her cycle for the embryo transfer at your fertility clinic.

5

Pregnancy & Birth

Nine months of shared milestones, clear communication, and preparation for the birth and parental rights process.

Real Cost Breakdown

What Does Surrogacy Really Cost?

The total cost of a gestational surrogacy journey in the United States typically falls between $80,000 and $180,000. Costs vary significantly based on your state, agency, surrogate, and clinic.

💡 The largest variable in total cost is often your surrogate's base compensation, which ranges from $30,000 to $60,000 depending on experience and location.

Common Questions

What Intended Parents Ask Most

What is the difference between gestational and traditional surrogacy? +
In gestational surrogacy — the most common type in the US today — the surrogate carries an embryo created via IVF using the intended parents' eggs and/or sperm, or donor material. She has no genetic connection to the baby. In traditional surrogacy, the surrogate uses her own egg, making her the genetic mother. Most agencies and attorneys no longer facilitate traditional surrogacy due to the significant legal and emotional complications involved.
How do we find the right surrogate? +
Most intended parents find their surrogate through a surrogacy agency, which pre-screens candidates, manages the matching process, and helps coordinate communication. Agencies typically present profiles of potential surrogates, and you have the opportunity to review backgrounds, preferences, and communication styles before agreeing to match. The process usually takes 1–4 months.
Do we need our own embryos, or can we use donor eggs and sperm? +
You do not need to use your own genetic material. Many intended parents use donor eggs, donor sperm, or both. This is especially common for same-sex male couples (who need an egg donor), single men, and women who cannot use their own eggs. The embryo is still transferred to the surrogate, who carries but is not genetically related to the child.
How much does surrogacy cost in the United States? +
Total costs typically range from $80,000 to $180,000, depending on your surrogate's compensation, agency fees, IVF costs, legal fees, and the cost of health insurance coverage. California and other major surrogacy states tend toward the higher end of the range. Our full cost guide breaks down every line item.
Are we the legal parents of the baby? +
Yes — in surrogacy-friendly states, intended parents are established as the legal parents through a pre-birth order (PBO) issued before the baby is born. In some states, a post-birth adoption or judgment is required instead. Your surrogacy attorney will file for parental rights based on the laws of whichever state your surrogate delivers in.
What states are best for surrogacy? +
California, Nevada, Washington, Colorado, Connecticut, Maine, New Hampshire, and Illinois are widely considered the most surrogacy-friendly states for intended parents. They allow pre-birth orders regardless of genetic connection, cover all family types, and have well-established legal frameworks. See our state-by-state guide for details on all 50 states.
