    Who pursues gestational surrogacy

    Surrogacy is for many different families

    🏥

    Medical conditions

    Uterine absence, severe fibroids, heart disease, prior cancer, or conditions where pregnancy poses serious health risk.

    👨‍👨‍👦

    Same-sex male couples

    Two male partners need both an egg donor and a gestational surrogate to have a biological child.

    👤

    Single men

    Single men pursue surrogacy through the same path as same-sex male couples: egg donor + surrogate.

    🔄

    Recurrent implantation failure

    When embryo quality is good but repeated IVF transfers have failed, a surrogate's uterus may be the solution.

    🌍

    International intended parents

    The US is the world's top surrogacy destination for international IPs — strong laws, high IVF success rates, accessible logistics.

    ⚧

    Transgender individuals

    Trans women and non-binary individuals who have undergone gender-affirming surgery may pursue surrogacy as a path to biological parenthood.

    The process

    How gestational surrogacy works, step by step

    A typical journey takes 12–18 months. Here are the major phases — each with its own professionals, costs, and decision points.

    1

    Research & agency selection

    Educate yourself, consult a reproductive attorney independently, evaluate 2–3 agencies before committing.

    2

    Surrogate matching

    Agency presents surrogate candidates. You review profiles, meet via video call, and confirm a match.

    3

    Legal contracts (GCA)

    Independent attorneys negotiate and execute the Gestational Carrier Agreement. No medical steps until this is signed.

    4

    Medical screening & embryo transfer

    Surrogate passes fertility clinic evaluation. FET protocol begins. Embryo transfer takes about 10 minutes.

    5

    Pregnancy & pre-birth order

    Your surrogate carries the pregnancy. Your attorney files for a pre-birth order during the second trimester.

    6

    Birth & legal finalization

    Your baby is born. With a PBO in place, you are named on the birth certificate from day one.

    Common questions

    Answers to the questions intended parents ask first

    Total costs typically range from $90,000 to $200,000, depending on surrogate compensation, agency fees, IVF costs, legal fees, and health insurance. California and major surrogacy markets tend toward the higher end. Our full cost guide breaks down every line item with low and high estimates.
    A pre-birth order is a court order issued during pregnancy that names intended parents as the legal parents of the expected child before birth. It directs the hospital to list intended parents on the birth certificate at delivery, eliminating the need for post-birth adoption. PBOs are available in surrogacy-friendly states for all family types.
    Yes. Same-sex male couples are one of the most common groups pursuing gestational surrogacy in the US. They need both an egg donor and a gestational surrogate. In surrogacy-friendly states, both partners can be named on the birth certificate via a pre-birth order, regardless of which partner is the genetic father.
    A typical gestational surrogacy journey takes 12–18 months from starting your agency search to birth: agency selection (1–2 months), matching (1–4 months), legal contracts (3–6 weeks), medical evaluation and embryo transfer (2–3 months), and the pregnancy (approximately 9 months). Build in a 3–6 month buffer for unexpected delays.
    No — not in gestational surrogacy. The embryo is created via IVF using the intended parents' or donors' genetic material and transferred to the surrogate's uterus. The surrogate carries and delivers the baby but has no genetic relationship to the child. This is the defining difference from traditional surrogacy, which is rarely practiced in the US today.
    A GCA is the comprehensive legal contract between intended parents and their gestational surrogate, covering compensation, medical decision-making, communication expectations, parental rights establishment, and all terms of the arrangement. It must be signed by both parties — each with independent legal counsel — before any fertility clinic will proceed with the embryo transfer.
